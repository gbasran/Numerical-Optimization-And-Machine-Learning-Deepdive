Linear Algebra Essentials

The matrix toolkit you need for optimization; eigenvalues, positive definiteness, matrix operations, and SVD

Part A: Eigenvalues; The DNA of a Matrix

Physical analogy; Resonant frequencies of a bell.

Pick up a bell and strike it. It vibrates at certain specific frequencies; its natural notes. A small bell rings at a high pitch; a big bell booms low. Those frequencies are not random; they are baked into the bell's shape and material. You cannot change the frequencies without physically changing the bell.

Every matrix has the same kind of "natural frequencies." They are called eigenvalues. The specific directions the bell vibrates along are the eigenvectors. The amount of vibration (amplitude) in each direction is the eigenvalue for that direction.

Why does this matter for optimization? Because the eigenvalues of a matrix tell you everything about its character:
• Are all eigenvalues positive? Then the matrix makes a bowl shape; perfect for minimization.
• Are some positive and some negative? Then the matrix makes a saddle; you are at neither a minimum nor a maximum.
• How spread out are the eigenvalues? That tells you how elongated the bowl is; which directly controls how fast algorithms converge (recall the condition number from Page 01).
The Eigenvalue Equation $$A\mathbf{v} = \lambda \mathbf{v}$$
$A$
a square matrix (like a Hessian → see Page 02)
$\mathbf{v}$
the eigenvector; a nonzero vector that does NOT change direction when $A$ acts on it
$\lambda$
the eigenvalue; the scalar that tells you how much $A$ stretches (or shrinks) $\mathbf{v}$

In words: multiply the matrix $A$ by the special vector $\mathbf{v}$, and you get back that same vector $\mathbf{v}$ scaled by $\lambda$. The direction doesn't change; only the magnitude.

How to Find Eigenvalues for a 2×2 Matrix

We need to solve $A\mathbf{v} = \lambda \mathbf{v}$. Rearrange: $$(A - \lambda I)\mathbf{v} = \mathbf{0}$$
$I$
the identity matrix $\begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix}$
$\lambda I$
subtract $\lambda$ from every diagonal entry of $A$
For a nonzero solution $\mathbf{v}$ to exist, the matrix $(A - \lambda I)$ must be "singular" (not invertible). That happens when its determinant is zero: $$\det(A - \lambda I) = 0$$ This gives us the characteristic polynomial; a polynomial in $\lambda$ whose roots are the eigenvalues.

For a 2×2 matrix $A = \begin{bmatrix} a & b \\ c & d \end{bmatrix}$:

$$\det\begin{bmatrix} a - \lambda & b \\ c & d - \lambda \end{bmatrix} = (a-\lambda)(d-\lambda) - bc = 0$$ This expands to: $$\lambda^2 - (a+d)\lambda + (ad - bc) = 0$$
$a + d$
the trace of $A$ (sum of diagonal entries)
$ad - bc$
the determinant of $A$
So the characteristic polynomial is: $\lambda^2 - \text{trace}(A)\,\lambda + \det(A) = 0$. Solve with the quadratic formula!

Hand Computation 1: Eigenvalues of a Diagonal Matrix

Find the eigenvalues of $A = \begin{bmatrix} 2 & 0 \\ 0 & 3 \end{bmatrix}$.

Step 1: Write the characteristic polynomial
$$\det(A - \lambda I) = \det\begin{bmatrix} 2 - \lambda & 0 \\ 0 & 3 - \lambda \end{bmatrix} = (2 - \lambda)(3 - \lambda) - (0)(0) = (2 - \lambda)(3 - \lambda)$$
Step 2: Set equal to zero and solve
$$(2 - \lambda)(3 - \lambda) = 0$$ So $\lambda_1 = 2$ and $\lambda_2 = 3$.
Result: The eigenvalues are $\lambda_1 = 2$ and $\lambda_2 = 3$; exactly the diagonal entries!
Shortcut: for a diagonal matrix, the eigenvalues are ALWAYS just the entries on the diagonal. No computation needed.

Hand Computation 2: Eigenvalues of the Quiz 5 CG Matrix

Find the eigenvalues of $A = \begin{bmatrix} 5 & 2 \\ 2 & 2 \end{bmatrix}$. This is the matrix from Conjugate Gradient problems.

Step 1: Set up $A - \lambda I$
$$A - \lambda I = \begin{bmatrix} 5 - \lambda & 2 \\ 2 & 2 - \lambda \end{bmatrix}$$
Step 2: Compute the determinant
$$\det(A - \lambda I) = (5 - \lambda)(2 - \lambda) - (2)(2)$$ Expand the first product: $$(5 - \lambda)(2 - \lambda) = 10 - 5\lambda - 2\lambda + \lambda^2 = \lambda^2 - 7\lambda + 10$$ Subtract the second product: $$\lambda^2 - 7\lambda + 10 - 4 = \lambda^2 - 7\lambda + 6$$
Step 3: Solve the characteristic polynomial
$$\lambda^2 - 7\lambda + 6 = 0$$ Factor: we need two numbers that multiply to 6 and add to 7. That is 1 and 6. $$(\lambda - 1)(\lambda - 6) = 0$$ So $\lambda_1 = 1$ and $\lambda_2 = 6$.
Result: $\lambda_1 = 1$, $\lambda_2 = 6$.
Sanity check: trace$(A) = 5 + 2 = 7 = 1 + 6 = \lambda_1 + \lambda_2$ ✓
det$(A) = 5 \cdot 2 - 2 \cdot 2 = 6 = 1 \cdot 6 = \lambda_1 \cdot \lambda_2$ ✓
Both eigenvalues are positive, so this matrix is positive definite (it makes a bowl). The condition number is $\kappa = \lambda_{\max}/\lambda_{\min} = 6/1 = 6$.
Quick eigenvalue sanity checks for 2×2 matrices:
• The sum of eigenvalues always equals the trace: $\lambda_1 + \lambda_2 = a_{11} + a_{22}$
• The product of eigenvalues always equals the determinant: $\lambda_1 \cdot \lambda_2 = \det(A)$
These give you two fast checks after computing eigenvalues.

Part B: Positive Definiteness; Is It a Bowl?

Physical analogy; Bowl vs saddle vs dome.

Imagine placing a marble on different surfaces:

Bowl (positive definite): Drop a marble anywhere and it rolls to the bottom. The surface curves UPWARD in every direction. No matter which way you walk from the lowest point, you go uphill. This is the shape we WANT for optimization; there is a clear unique minimum.

Dome (negative definite): Place a marble on top and it rolls off in every direction. The surface curves DOWNWARD everywhere. This is a maximum , the opposite of what we want when minimizing.

Saddle (indefinite): Think of a horse saddle or a mountain pass. Walk north-south and you go downhill. Walk east-west and you go uphill. The marble might sit momentarily at the center, but it is not at a minimum or maximum. It is a saddle point.

The eigenvalues of a matrix tell you which of these shapes you have. All positive eigenvalues = bowl. All negative = dome. Mixed signs = saddle.
Formal Definition: Positive Definiteness $$\text{A symmetric matrix } A \text{ is positive definite (PD) if } \mathbf{x}^T A \mathbf{x} > 0 \text{ for all } \mathbf{x} \neq \mathbf{0}$$
$\mathbf{x}$
any nonzero vector (any direction you might walk)
$\mathbf{x}^T A \mathbf{x}$
a scalar number; the "curvature" in direction $\mathbf{x}$
$> 0$
the curvature is positive (upward) in EVERY direction

Equivalent characterizations (any one is enough):

  1. All eigenvalues of $A$ are strictly positive: $\lambda_i > 0$ for all $i$
  2. $\mathbf{x}^T A \mathbf{x} > 0$ for all $\mathbf{x} \neq \mathbf{0}$
  3. For 2×2: the top-left entry $a_{11} > 0$ AND the determinant $\det(A) > 0$
Classification Table for Symmetric Matrices
ClassificationEigenvalue ConditionSurface ShapeOptimization Meaning

Negative Definite (ND)All $\lambda_i < 0$DomeLocal maximum
IndefiniteMixed signsSaddleSaddle point

The 2×2 shortcut (very useful on exams):
For a symmetric 2×2 matrix $\begin{bmatrix} a & b \\ b & d \end{bmatrix}$:
• PD if $a > 0$ and $ad - b^2 > 0$
• ND if $a < 0$ and $ad - b^2 > 0$
• Indefinite if $ad - b^2 < 0$
• Semidefinite / Inconclusive if $ad - b^2 = 0$
This is faster than computing eigenvalues!

Hand Computation: Positive Definiteness Check; Quiz 2 Hessian

Consider the Hessian $H = \begin{bmatrix} 12x_1 - 6 - 12x_2 & -12x_1 + 12x_2 + 6 \\ -12x_1 + 12x_2 + 6 & 12x_2 + 6 \end{bmatrix}$ from Quiz 2.

We evaluate at the stationary point $(0, 0)$:

Step 1: Plug in $x_1 = 0, x_2 = 0$
$$H(0,0) = \begin{bmatrix} 0 - 6 - 0 & 0 + 0 + 6 \\ 0 + 0 + 6 & 0 + 6 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} -6 & 6 \\ 6 & 6 \end{bmatrix}$$
Step 2: Use the 2×2 shortcut
$a_{11} = -6 < 0$ and $\det(H) = (-6)(6) - (6)(6) = -36 - 36 = -72 < 0$.

Since $\det < 0$, the matrix is indefinite (mixed eigenvalues).
Step 3: Verify with eigenvalues
Characteristic polynomial: $\lambda^2 - (−6+6)\lambda + (−72) = \lambda^2 - 72 = 0$
Wait, let us be more careful: trace $= -6 + 6 = 0$, det $= -72$. $$\lambda^2 - 0\cdot\lambda + (-72) = \lambda^2 - 72 = 0$$ $$\lambda = \pm\sqrt{72} = \pm 6\sqrt{2} \approx \pm 8.49$$
Result: Eigenvalues are $\lambda_1 = 6\sqrt{2} > 0$ and $\lambda_2 = -6\sqrt{2} < 0$. Mixed signs = indefinite = saddle point at $(0,0)$.
This matches: the 2×2 shortcut told us $\det < 0$ which means indefinite, and indeed one eigenvalue is positive and one is negative. The point $(0,0)$ is a saddle point → see Page 04 for the full classification.

Why Does Positive Definiteness Keep Appearing?

Positive definiteness is THE central concept in optimization. It shows up everywhere:

  • Second-order sufficient conditions (Page 04): a stationary point is a local minimizer if the Hessian is PD there.
  • Convexity (Page 05): a function is convex iff its Hessian is PSD everywhere.
  • Conjugate Gradient (Page 10): linear CG only works when $A$ is PD (the system $Ax = b$ must come from a bowl-shaped quadratic).
  • BFGS (Page 12): the BFGS update formula is specifically designed to keep the Hessian approximation PD at every step.
  • Trust regions (Page 09): the trust-region subproblem needs PD for a clean solution.

If you understand nothing else from linear algebra, understand this: PD = bowl = minimum = good for optimization.

Part C: Matrix Operations That Matter

These operations appear constantly in the algorithms on later pages. If you are rusty, work through these by hand until they feel natural.

Matrix-Vector Multiply (2×2 case) $$\begin{bmatrix} a & b \\ c & d \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} x \\ y \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} ax + by \\ cx + dy \end{bmatrix}$$

How to read it: Each entry of the result is a dot product of a row of the matrix with the vector.

  • First entry: (row 1 of $A$) $\cdot$ (vector) $= ax + by$
  • Second entry: (row 2 of $A$) $\cdot$ (vector) $= cx + dy$

Example: Matrix-Vector Multiply

Compute $A\mathbf{x}$ where $A = \begin{bmatrix} 5 & 2 \\ 2 & 2 \end{bmatrix}$ and $\mathbf{x} = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ -3 \end{bmatrix}$.

Row 1 dot vector:
$(5)(1) + (2)(-3) = 5 - 6 = -1$
Row 2 dot vector:
$(2)(1) + (2)(-3) = 2 - 6 = -4$
Result: $A\mathbf{x} = \begin{bmatrix} -1 \\ -4 \end{bmatrix}$
Outer Product: $\mathbf{v}\mathbf{w}^T$ = a Matrix

An outer product takes two vectors and produces a matrix. This is NOT the same as the dot product (which produces a scalar).

$$\begin{bmatrix} v_1 \\ v_2 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} w_1 & w_2 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} v_1 w_1 & v_1 w_2 \\ v_2 w_1 & v_2 w_2 \end{bmatrix}$$
$\mathbf{v}$
a column vector ($n \times 1$)
$\mathbf{w}^T$
a row vector ($1 \times n$); the transpose of a column vector
$\mathbf{v}\mathbf{w}^T$
an $n \times n$ matrix; each entry is $v_i w_j$

This operation appears in the BFGS update formula (Page 12) and the SR1 update (Page 13). Both build rank-1 or rank-2 corrections to the Hessian approximation using outer products.

Example: Outer Product

Compute $\mathbf{v}\mathbf{w}^T$ where $\mathbf{v} = \begin{bmatrix} 3 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix}$ and $\mathbf{w} = \begin{bmatrix} 2 \\ -1 \end{bmatrix}$.

Form the outer product:
$$\begin{bmatrix} 3 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} 2 & -1 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} (3)(2) & (3)(-1) \\ (1)(2) & (1)(-1) \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 6 & -3 \\ 2 & -1 \end{bmatrix}$$
Compare: the dot product $\mathbf{v}^T \mathbf{w} = (3)(2) + (1)(-1) = 5$ is a single number. The outer product is a whole matrix. Know the difference!
2×2 Matrix Inverse $$\begin{bmatrix} a & b \\ c & d \end{bmatrix}^{-1} = \frac{1}{ad - bc}\begin{bmatrix} d & -b \\ -c & a \end{bmatrix}$$
$ad - bc$
the determinant of the matrix; it must be nonzero for the inverse to exist
recipe
swap the diagonals ($a \leftrightarrow d$), negate the off-diagonals ($b \to -b$, $c \to -c$), divide everything by the determinant

You need this for Newton's method: the Newton step is $\mathbf{p} = -(\nabla^2 f)^{-1} \nabla f$, which requires inverting the Hessian. See Page 07.

Hand Computation: Invert the Quiz 4 Hessian

Invert $H = \begin{bmatrix} 28 & -2 \\ -2 & 2 \end{bmatrix}$.

Step 1: Compute the determinant
$$\det(H) = (28)(2) - (-2)(-2) = 56 - 4 = 52$$
Step 2: Apply the formula
$$H^{-1} = \frac{1}{52}\begin{bmatrix} 2 & 2 \\ 2 & 28 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 2/52 & 2/52 \\ 2/52 & 28/52 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 1/26 & 1/26 \\ 1/26 & 7/13 \end{bmatrix}$$
Result: $H^{-1} = \frac{1}{52}\begin{bmatrix} 2 & 2 \\ 2 & 28 \end{bmatrix}$
Sanity check: $HH^{-1}$ should equal $I$. Row 1: $(28)(1/26) + (-2)(1/26) = 28/26 - 2/26 = 26/26 = 1$ ✓, and $(28)(1/26) + (-2)(7/13) = 28/26 - 14/13 = 14/13 - 14/13 = 0$ ✓. The inverse is correct.
Inner product (dot product) vs outer product; keep them straight:
• $\mathbf{v}^T \mathbf{w}$ = dot product = a scalar number (one value)
• $\mathbf{v}\mathbf{w}^T$ = outer product = an $n \times n$ matrix
The only difference is the order: $\mathbf{v}^T\mathbf{w}$ (row times column = scalar) vs $\mathbf{v}\mathbf{w}^T$ (column times row = matrix).

Part D: SVD and Matrix Norms (Conceptual)

Physical analogy; Any transformation = rotate, stretch, rotate.

Think about what a matrix does to a circle of vectors. It stretches the circle into an ellipse. The SVD (Singular Value Decomposition) breaks this action into three simple steps:

1. Rotate the circle (align it with special directions)
2. Stretch along each axis (by the singular values $\sigma_1, \sigma_2, \ldots$)
3. Rotate again (orient the ellipse in output space)

No matter how complicated the matrix, its action is just rotate-stretch-rotate. The singular values tell you how much stretching happens in each direction.
SVD: $A = U \Sigma V^T$
$U$
rotation matrix for the output (columns are left singular vectors)
$\Sigma$
diagonal matrix of singular values $\sigma_1 \geq \sigma_2 \geq \cdots \geq 0$
$V^T$
rotation matrix for the input (rows are right singular vectors)

For a symmetric PD matrix $A$, the singular values equal the eigenvalues, and $U = V$. So for the matrices in our optimization course, SVD and eigendecomposition are essentially the same thing.

Condition Number (Revisited from Page 01) $$\kappa(A) = \frac{\sigma_{\max}}{\sigma_{\min}} = \frac{\lambda_{\max}}{\lambda_{\min}} \quad \text{(for symmetric PD matrices)}$$
$\sigma_{\max}$
the largest singular value; the maximum stretching
$\sigma_{\min}$
the smallest singular value; the minimum stretching
$\kappa(A)$
the condition number; the ratio of maximum to minimum stretching

What it means: A large condition number means the matrix stretches MUCH more in one direction than another. The bowl is very elongated. This makes optimization algorithms converge slowly because they zigzag back and forth.

Example: For our Quiz 5 matrix with $\lambda_1 = 1, \lambda_2 = 6$: $\kappa = 6$. The bowl is 6 times wider in one direction than the other. Not too bad. If $\kappa = 1000$, the bowl is extremely elongated and gradient descent would be painfully slow.

Matrix Norms (Brief)

Just as vectors have norms (magnitudes), matrices do too. The most common:

NormDefinitionMeaning
$\|A\|_2$ (spectral)$\sigma_{\max}(A)$Maximum stretching by $A$
$\|A\|_F$ (Frobenius)$\sqrt{\sum_{i,j} a_{ij}^2}$Like the L2 norm but for all matrix entries

You mostly need matrix norms for convergence proofs (Page 01) and when comparing matrices in BFGS/SR1 updates. For exam purposes, knowing $\|A\|_2 = \sigma_{\max}$ is the key fact.

Glossary

Eigenvalue ($\lambda$)
A scalar such that $A\mathbf{v} = \lambda\mathbf{v}$ for some nonzero vector $\mathbf{v}$. The "natural frequency" of the matrix in direction $\mathbf{v}$.
Eigenvector ($\mathbf{v}$)
A nonzero vector that only gets scaled (not rotated) when multiplied by $A$. The "natural direction" associated with eigenvalue $\lambda$.
Characteristic Polynomial
$\det(A - \lambda I) = 0$. A polynomial whose roots are the eigenvalues of $A$. For $n \times n$ matrices, it is degree $n$.
Positive Definite (PD)
A symmetric matrix with all eigenvalues strictly positive. Geometrically: a bowl. $\mathbf{x}^T A \mathbf{x} > 0$ for all $\mathbf{x} \neq 0$.
Negative Definite (ND)
A symmetric matrix with all eigenvalues strictly negative. Geometrically: a dome (upside-down bowl).
Indefinite
A symmetric matrix with both positive and negative eigenvalues. Geometrically: a saddle shape.
Positive Semidefinite (PSD)
A symmetric matrix with all eigenvalues $\geq 0$ (some may be zero). Like PD but the bowl may have flat directions.
Symmetric Matrix
A matrix equal to its own transpose: $A = A^T$. Equivalently, $a_{ij} = a_{ji}$. All Hessians are symmetric (if the function is smooth enough).
Matrix Inverse ($A^{-1}$)
The matrix such that $AA^{-1} = A^{-1}A = I$. Only exists if $\det(A) \neq 0$. For 2×2: swap diagonals, negate off-diagonals, divide by det.
Determinant ($\det A$)
For 2×2: $ad - bc$. Equals the product of all eigenvalues. If $\det = 0$, the matrix is singular (not invertible).
Trace ($\text{tr}(A)$)
Sum of diagonal entries. Equals the sum of all eigenvalues. For 2×2: $a + d$.
Outer Product ($\mathbf{v}\mathbf{w}^T$)
A column vector times a row vector, producing an $n \times n$ matrix. Used in BFGS and SR1 update formulas.
SVD (Singular Value Decomposition)
$A = U\Sigma V^T$. Decomposes any matrix into rotate-stretch-rotate. The diagonal entries of $\Sigma$ are the singular values.
Condition Number ($\kappa$)
$\kappa = \sigma_{\max}/\sigma_{\min}$. Measures how "elongated" the bowl is. Large $\kappa$ means slow convergence for iterative methods.
Matrix Norm
A way to measure the "size" of a matrix. The spectral norm $\|A\|_2 = \sigma_{\max}$ is the most common in optimization theory.
