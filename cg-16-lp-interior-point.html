Linear Programming & Interior Point Methods

When everything is linear; the simplest constrained problems and the elegant barrier approach to solving them

PART A

Linear Programming; The Simplest Constrained Problem

Analogy: A tilted table inside a box. Imagine you have a flat, tilted tabletop (that is your objective function; it is linear, so it is just a flat plane that slopes in one direction). Now imagine this table is sitting inside a box with flat walls (those are your constraints; also all linear, forming a shape with flat sides called a polyhedron).

Where on this table, inside the box, is the surface lowest? Since the table is perfectly flat (no curves, no bumps), the lowest point cannot be in the middle of the box; the tilt always pushes you toward one edge. In fact, the lowest point is always at a corner (vertex) of the box. This is the fundamental property of linear programming: the optimal solution is always at a vertex of the feasible region.

Why? Because if you are anywhere in the interior, you can always slide along the tilt direction and decrease the objective further, until you hit a wall. Then you can slide along that wall until you hit a corner. Only at corners can you not improve further in any feasible direction.

Standard Form of a Linear Program (LP)

$$\min \; c^T x \quad \text{subject to} \quad Ax \leq b, \quad x \geq 0$$
$c \in \mathbb{R}^n$
The cost vector; tells you how much each variable contributes to the objective. It defines the "tilt" of the table.
$x \in \mathbb{R}^n$
The decision variables; what we are choosing. Must be non-negative ($x \geq 0$).
$A \in \mathbb{R}^{m \times n}$
The constraint matrix; each row is one constraint. Together with $b$, they define the walls of the box.
$b \in \mathbb{R}^m$
The right-hand side; the limits for each constraint.
$c^T x$
The objective function; a simple dot product (recall from Page 03). This is linear: no squares, no products of variables, just a weighted sum.

What makes LP special? Both the objective $c^T x$ and the constraints $Ax \leq b$ are linear. This is the simplest possible constrained optimization problem. No curves, no curvature, no Hessians. Just flat surfaces and straight lines. This extreme simplicity is what makes LP solvable so efficiently.

Why LP matters for this course

LP is not just a toy example; it is one of the most practically important optimization problems in the world. Airlines use LP to schedule crews. Companies use LP to plan supply chains. Finance uses LP for portfolio optimization. The KKT conditions you learned on Page 15 simplify beautifully for LP because everything is linear. And the interior point methods we will learn here connect back to the barrier idea, which is one of the great achievements in optimization theory.

Key property: The feasible region of an LP is a polyhedron (a shape with flat faces). Since the objective is linear (a flat tilt), the optimal solution always occurs at a vertex. This is fundamentally different from nonlinear optimization where the optimum can be anywhere.

PART B

KKT Conditions for LP; Everything Simplifies

Connecting back to Page 15: On Page 15, we learned the four KKT conditions for general constrained optimization: stationarity, primal feasibility, dual feasibility, and complementary slackness. For LP, these conditions simplify dramatically because everything is linear; there are no Hessians, no curvature, just straight lines and flat planes.

Consider the LP in standard equality form (we convert inequalities to equalities by adding slack variables $s \geq 0$):

LP in Equality Form

$$\min \; c^T x \quad \text{subject to} \quad Ax = b, \quad x \geq 0$$

The inequality $Ax \leq b$ becomes $Ax + s = b$ with $s \geq 0$, then we absorb $s$ into $x$.

The Lagrangian (recall from Page 15) for this LP is:

$$\mathcal{L}(x, \lambda, \mu) = c^T x - \lambda^T(Ax - b) - \mu^T x$$
$\lambda \in \mathbb{R}^m$
Multipliers for the equality constraints $Ax = b$ (no sign restriction)
$\mu \in \mathbb{R}^n$
Multipliers for $x \geq 0$ (must have $\mu \geq 0$ by dual feasibility)

The KKT conditions for LP become:

KKT Conditions for LP

1. Stationarity: $\nabla_x \mathcal{L} = c - A^T \lambda - \mu = 0$, so $\mu = c - A^T \lambda$

The gradient of the Lagrangian with respect to $x$ is zero. Since everything is linear, this just gives us $\mu$ in terms of $\lambda$.

2. Primal feasibility: $Ax = b$ and $x \geq 0$

The point must satisfy all constraints.

3. Dual feasibility: $\mu \geq 0$, which means $c - A^T \lambda \geq 0$

The multipliers for the bound constraints must be non-negative.

4. Complementary slackness: $\mu_i x_i = 0$ for each $i$

For each variable: either $x_i = 0$ (variable is at its bound) or $\mu_i = 0$ (the bound is not "active"). This means at the optimum, most variables are either zero or have zero multiplier.

Why does this simplify?

In general nonlinear optimization (Page 15), the stationarity condition involves gradients of nonlinear functions, which require computing partial derivatives and possibly Hessians. For LP, $\nabla_x (c^T x) = c$ is just a constant vector; no computation needed. The constraint gradients $\nabla_x (a_i^T x) = a_i$ are also constants. So the KKT system reduces to a simple linear system, not a nonlinear one. This is why LP is so much easier to solve than general nonlinear constrained optimization.

Connection to Quiz 8

Quiz 8 asked you to solve an LP using KKT conditions. The problem $\min -3x_1 - 2x_2$ subject to $x_1 + x_2 \leq 4$, $x_1 + 3x_2 \leq 6$, $x_1, x_2 \geq 0$ was worked in full on Page 15. The same problem viewed through the LP lens here: the feasible region is a polygon, and the optimal solution $(3, 1)$ or $(4, 0)$ is at a vertex, exactly as LP theory predicts.

PART C

Interior Point Methods; Going Through the Middle

Analogy: Repulsive force fields near the walls. There are two fundamentally different strategies for solving LP:

Strategy 1 (Simplex method): Walk along the EDGES of the feasible region, hopping from corner to corner, always moving to a better corner. This is like exploring a room by walking along the walls. It works well in practice but can visit exponentially many corners in the worst case.

Strategy 2 (Interior point): Stay INSIDE the room and create an invisible "repulsive force field" near every wall. This force pushes you away from the walls, keeping you safely in the interior. Your objective (the tilt of the table) pulls you toward the optimal corner, but the force field keeps you from actually touching the walls. Now here is the elegant trick: you gradually WEAKEN the force field. As the repulsion gets weaker and weaker, you drift closer and closer to the optimal corner. In the limit (force field strength approaches zero), you arrive at the exact optimal vertex.

The "force field" is mathematically implemented as a logarithmic barrier function: $-\mu \sum \ln(x_i)$. When $x_i$ is close to zero (near a wall), $\ln(x_i) \to -\infty$, creating a massive penalty that pushes you away. The parameter $\mu$ controls the strength of the force field.

The Barrier Problem

Instead of solving the LP directly, we solve a sequence of barrier problems:

$$\min_x \; c^T x \;-\; \mu \sum_{i=1}^{n} \ln(x_i) \quad \text{subject to} \quad Ax = b$$
$c^T x$
The original LP objective (the tilt pulling you toward the optimum)
$-\mu \sum \ln(x_i)$
The log barrier; the repulsive force field. Each $\ln(x_i)$ term goes to $-\infty$ as $x_i \to 0^+$, so the negative sign creates a penalty that pushes $x_i$ away from zero.
$\mu > 0$
The barrier parameter; controls force field strength. Large $\mu$ = strong repulsion (stay far from walls). Small $\mu$ = weak repulsion (can approach walls closely).
$Ax = b$
The equality constraints remain unchanged.

Key insight: This barrier problem has NO inequality constraints ($x \geq 0$). The log barrier automatically enforces $x > 0$ because $\ln(x_i)$ is only defined for $x_i > 0$. We have traded the hard constraint $x \geq 0$ for a smooth penalty term.

Why use a logarithmic barrier?

We could use any function that blows up as $x_i \to 0$, but the logarithm has special properties that make Newton's method (recall from Page 07) work beautifully on the barrier problem. The Hessian of $-\sum \ln(x_i)$ is the diagonal matrix $\text{diag}(1/x_i^2)$, which is always positive definite (recall from Page 03: diagonal matrix with positive entries is PD). This means the barrier problem is always well-conditioned for Newton's method.

PART D

The Central Path; A Highway to the Optimum

Analogy: Adjusting a thermostat. Imagine you are trying to find the coldest spot in a room (the optimum), but there are heaters near every wall (the barriers). With the heaters on full blast ($\mu$ large), the coldest spot is right in the center of the room; far from all heaters. As you slowly turn down the heaters ($\mu$ decreasing), the coldest spot drifts toward the naturally coldest corner of the room (the LP optimum). The path traced by the coldest spot as you dial down the heaters is the central path.

The Central Path

For each value of $\mu > 0$, the barrier problem has a unique solution $x(\mu)$. As $\mu$ varies from large to small, these solutions trace out a curve called the central path:

$$\{x(\mu) : \mu > 0\}$$

As $\mu \to 0$, the central path converges to the optimal solution $x^*$ of the original LP.

The interior point algorithm works by approximately following the central path. At each step, it uses Newton's method to solve the barrier problem for the current $\mu$, then reduces $\mu$ by a fixed factor (typically $\mu_{\text{new}} = 0.1 \cdot \mu_{\text{old}}$).

PART E

Modified KKT: Relaxed Complementarity

The key mathematical insight: The KKT conditions for the original LP require exact complementary slackness: $\mu_i x_i = 0$. This is a hard, non-smooth condition (one of the two must be exactly zero). The barrier approach relaxes this to $\mu_i x_i = \sigma$ for a small positive $\sigma$ (related to the barrier parameter). This turns a hard combinatorial problem into a smooth one that Newton's method can handle.

KKT for the Barrier Problem

The optimality conditions for the barrier subproblem are:

1. Stationarity: $c - A^T \lambda - \mu_{\text{mult}} = 0$

2. Primal feasibility: $Ax = b$, $x > 0$ (strictly positive; we stay in the interior)

3. Relaxed complementarity: $\mu_{\text{mult},i} \cdot x_i = \sigma$ for each $i$

Instead of $\mu_i x_i = 0$ (exact complementarity), we have $\mu_i x_i = \sigma > 0$. As $\sigma \to 0$, this approaches exact complementarity.

4. Dual feasibility: $\mu_{\text{mult}} > 0$ (strictly positive in the interior)

Why relaxation works

Exact complementary slackness ($\mu_i x_i = 0$) is hard because it has a combinatorial structure: for each $i$, you must decide which of $\mu_i$ or $x_i$ is zero. With $n$ variables, there are $2^n$ possible combinations to check. The relaxed version ($\mu_i x_i = \sigma$) is a smooth system of equations that Newton's method can solve efficiently. As we decrease $\sigma$ toward zero, the solution of the relaxed system converges to the solution of the original LP.

PART F

The Interior Point Algorithm

Interior Point Method for LP

Input: $c$, $A$, $b$, initial strictly feasible $(x_0, \lambda_0, \mu_0)$ with $x_0 > 0$, $\mu_0 > 0$
Parameters: $\sigma \in (0,1)$ (centering parameter, typically 0.1–0.5)
 
for $k = 0, 1, 2, \ldots$:
1. Compute barrier parameter: $\tau_k = \sigma \cdot \frac{x_k^T \mu_k}{n}$ ← the "average" complementarity, reduced by factor $\sigma$
2. Solve the Newton system for the relaxed KKT conditions ← uses Newton's method from Page 07
3. Take a step along the Newton direction (with step size to stay feasible: $x > 0$, $\mu > 0$)
4. Update: $x_{k+1}$, $\lambda_{k+1}$, $\mu_{k+1}$
5. if $x_k^T \mu_k / n < \epsilon$: stop ← complementarity is close to zero, we have converged

Convergence speed: Interior point methods converge in $O(\sqrt{n})$ iterations, where each iteration solves a linear system of size $n$. Total cost: roughly $O(n^{3.5})$ operations. This is polynomial time; guaranteed to be fast regardless of the problem structure. The simplex method, by contrast, can take exponential time in the worst case (though it is usually fast in practice).

Conceptual Example: 2D LP

Consider: $\min -x_1 - x_2$ subject to $x_1 + x_2 \leq 1$, $x_1, x_2 \geq 0$.

The feasible region is a triangle with vertices $(0,0)$, $(1,0)$, $(0,1)$. The optimal solution is on the edge between $(1,0)$ and $(0,1)$.

With large $\mu$ (strong barrier):

The barrier solution is near the center of the triangle: $x(\mu) \approx (1/3, 1/3)$. The log barrier pushes away from all three walls equally.

With medium $\mu$:

The solution moves toward the optimal edge: $x(\mu) \approx (0.45, 0.45)$. The objective's pull toward the edge starts overcoming the barrier's push.

With small $\mu$:

The solution is very close to the optimal edge: $x(\mu) \approx (0.49, 0.49)$. The barrier is weak enough that we are nearly at the optimum.

As $\mu \to 0$:

$x^* = (0.5, 0.5)$; the point on the optimal edge closest to the center of the central path.

PART G

Simplex vs Interior Point; Two Philosophies

Property Simplex Method Interior Point Method
Strategy Walk along edges, corner to corner Move through the interior, approaching optimal corner
Path On the boundary (vertices and edges) Strictly in the interior (central path)
Worst-case iterations Exponential ($2^n$ possible vertices) Polynomial ($O(\sqrt{n})$ iterations)
Typical iterations $2m$ to $3m$ (often fast in practice) $20$ to $50$ (nearly constant!)
Cost per iteration Cheap (pivot operation) Expensive (solve Newton system)
Best for Small-medium LPs, warm starts Large sparse LPs
Historical Dantzig, 1947 Karmarkar, 1984

Why interior point methods matter beyond LP

The barrier/interior point idea generalizes far beyond linear programming. It applies to quadratic programming (QP), semidefinite programming (SDP), and general nonlinear constrained optimization. The core insight; replace hard constraints with smooth penalties and gradually tighten; is one of the most powerful ideas in optimization. Penalty methods and augmented Lagrangian methods (Nocedal & Wright Chapters 17-18, not covered in our course) are extensions of this same philosophy.

PART H

How Everything Connects

Let us step back and see how LP and interior point methods connect to everything else we have learned:

  • KKT conditions (Page 15) are the optimality conditions for constrained problems. For LP, they simplify to a linear system.
  • Newton's method (Page 07) is used at every step of the interior point method to solve the barrier subproblem.
  • Positive definiteness (Page 03) of the barrier Hessian ensures Newton's method works well.
  • Complementary slackness (Page 15) is what the relaxation parameter $\sigma$ gradually enforces.
  • Convexity (Page 05); LP is a convex problem, so any local optimum is global. The central path always leads to THE answer.
  • Convergence rates (Page 01); interior point methods achieve polynomial-time convergence, a theoretically stronger guarantee than simplex.

Glossary

Linear Program (LP)
An optimization problem where both the objective function and all constraints are linear. The simplest form of constrained optimization. Standard form: $\min c^T x$ subject to $Ax \leq b$, $x \geq 0$.
Feasible Region
The set of all points $x$ that satisfy all constraints. For an LP, this is a polyhedron (a shape with flat faces formed by the intersection of half-spaces).
Vertex (Corner Point)
A corner of the feasible region where constraint boundaries intersect. LP optimal solutions always occur at vertices (or along an edge connecting two equally-optimal vertices).
Simplex Method
An algorithm that moves along edges of the feasible polyhedron from vertex to vertex, improving the objective at each step. Invented by George Dantzig in 1947. Fast in practice but exponential worst-case.
Interior Point Method
An algorithm that moves through the interior of the feasible region, using a barrier function to stay away from boundaries. Polynomial-time guarantee. Invented by Narendra Karmarkar in 1984.
Log Barrier Function
The term $-\mu \sum \ln(x_i)$ added to the objective. It creates a "repulsive force" near the boundaries $x_i = 0$, preventing the iterates from leaving the feasible region. The parameter $\mu$ controls the barrier strength.
Barrier Parameter ($\mu$)
A positive scalar that controls the strength of the log barrier. Large $\mu$ keeps iterates far from boundaries; as $\mu \to 0$, the barrier weakens and the solution approaches the true LP optimum.
Central Path
The curve traced by the barrier problem solutions $x(\mu)$ as $\mu$ varies from $+\infty$ to $0$. It starts in the "center" of the feasible region and converges to the LP optimal vertex.
Relaxed Complementarity
The condition $\mu_i x_i = \sigma$ (for $\sigma > 0$) instead of exact complementary slackness $\mu_i x_i = 0$. This turns the combinatorial KKT conditions into a smooth system solvable by Newton's method.
Slack Variable
A non-negative variable $s$ added to convert inequality $a^T x \leq b$ into equality $a^T x + s = b$ with $s \geq 0$. This is a standard technique for putting LPs into equality form.
Polynomial Time
An algorithm whose running time grows as a polynomial function of the problem size $n$ (e.g., $O(n^{3.5})$). Interior point methods are polynomial; simplex is not (in the worst case). In practice, both are fast for typical problems.
Polyhedron
A shape formed by the intersection of finitely many half-spaces (linear inequalities). The feasible region of an LP is always a polyhedron. In 2D, polyhedra are polygons. In 3D, they are shapes like cubes and tetrahedra.
