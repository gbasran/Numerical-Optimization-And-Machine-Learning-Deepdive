SR1 and DFP Methods

Two more quasi-Newton updates; SR1 is simpler (rank 1), DFP is BFGS's older sibling (rank 2)

Part A: SR1; Symmetric Rank-One Update

Physical analogy; tuning one string at a time.

On Page 12, we saw that BFGS updates the Hessian approximation with a rank-2 correction; it adjusts two "dimensions" of the curvature map each step. Think of it like tuning a piano: BFGS adjusts two strings at once to bring them into harmony.

SR1 (Symmetric Rank-One) is simpler: it only adjusts one string per step. The correction matrix has rank 1 instead of rank 2. This makes the formula shorter and easier to compute, but there is a trade-off: SR1 does NOT guarantee the curvature map stays bowl-shaped (positive definite). Sometimes the map can develop "saddle" directions, which means the search direction might not point downhill.

Despite this, SR1 has a remarkable property: it can learn the exact curvature map ($H_k = Q^{-1}$) in fewer steps than BFGS on some problems. In the worked example below, SR1 recovers $Q^{-1}$ in just ONE iteration.
SR1 inverse Hessian update: $$H_{k+1} = H_k + \frac{(s_k - H_k y_k)(s_k - H_k y_k)^T}{(s_k - H_k y_k)^T y_k}$$
$H_k$
current inverse Hessian approximation (recall from Page 12)
$s_k = x_{k+1} - x_k$
step taken (same as in BFGS)
$y_k = g_{k+1} - g_k$
gradient change (same as in BFGS)
$H_k y_k$
what our current map predicts the step should have been, given the observed gradient change. If $H_k$ were perfect ($H_k = \nabla^2 f^{-1}$), then $H_k y_k$ would equal $s_k$ exactly (by the secant condition).
$s_k - H_k y_k$
the prediction error; how wrong our map was. If zero, the map already predicted perfectly and no update is needed.
$(s_k - H_k y_k)(s_k - H_k y_k)^T$
an outer product of the error with itself; a rank-1 matrix (one "dimension" of correction)
$(s_k - H_k y_k)^T y_k$
a scalar (the denominator); projects the error onto the gradient change direction

Key structural observation: The numerator is $(s_k - H_k y_k)(s_k - H_k y_k)^T$, which is a rank-1 matrix (outer product of a vector with itself). So the entire correction is rank 1. Compare with BFGS, which subtracts one rank-1 matrix and adds another, giving a rank-2 correction.

Why SR1 Satisfies the Secant Condition

Lets verify. The secant condition for the inverse is $H_{k+1} s_k = ??? $. Actually, the secant condition is $H_{k+1} y_k = s_k$. Lets check:

$$H_{k+1} y_k = H_k y_k + \frac{(s_k - H_k y_k)(s_k - H_k y_k)^T y_k}{(s_k - H_k y_k)^T y_k}$$

The fraction simplifies: $(s_k - H_k y_k)^T y_k$ in the numerator cancels the denominator, leaving:

$$= H_k y_k + (s_k - H_k y_k) = s_k \quad \checkmark$$

So $H_{k+1} y_k = s_k$, confirming the secant condition is satisfied.

Part B: SR1 Safeguard

The problem: the denominator can blow up

The SR1 update has denominator $(s_k - H_k y_k)^T y_k$. If this is zero or very close to zero, we are dividing by (nearly) zero, and the update matrix would have enormous entries; numerical disaster.

This happens when the prediction error $s_k - H_k y_k$ is (nearly) perpendicular to $y_k$. In that case, the gradient change doesnt give us useful information in the error direction, and we should skip the update.

The safeguard rule: $$\text{Skip the SR1 update if } \quad |(s_k - H_k y_k)^T y_k| \lt r \, \|s_k - H_k y_k\| \cdot \|y_k\|$$
$r$
a small positive number, typically $r = 10^{-8}$
$|\cdot|$
absolute value of the scalar
$\|s_k - H_k y_k\|$
length of the prediction error vector
$\|y_k\|$
length of the gradient change vector

The right-hand side is $r$ times the product of the two vector lengths. By the Cauchy-Schwarz inequality, $|(s_k - H_k y_k)^T y_k| \leq \|s_k - H_k y_k\| \cdot \|y_k\|$, so this condition triggers when the cosine of the angle between the two vectors is smaller than $r$; i.e., they are nearly perpendicular.

When the safeguard triggers, we simply keep $H_{k+1} = H_k$ (dont update) and move on to the next iteration.

SR1 Does NOT Preserve Positive Definiteness

Unlike BFGS, the SR1 update can produce a matrix $H_{k+1}$ that is NOT positive definite, even if $H_k$ was. This means the search direction $p_k = -H_k g_k$ might not be a descent direction.

When is this OK? SR1 is most commonly used inside a trust-region framework (recall from Page 09). In a trust region, the step is bounded by the trust radius $\Delta_k$, so even if $H_k$ produces a bad direction, the step is kept small enough to be safe. The trust region acts as a safety net.

When is this a problem? If you use SR1 with a line search (instead of a trust region), you need to check that $p_k$ is a descent direction ($g_k^T p_k \lt 0$). If not, you fall back to steepest descent.

Summary of SR1 trade-offs:
  • Pro: Simpler formula (rank-1 vs rank-2)
  • Pro: Can learn $Q^{-1}$ faster on some problems
  • Pro: Can capture non-convex curvature (useful for saddle points)
  • Con: No positive definiteness guarantee; needs trust region or safeguard
  • Con: Denominator can vanish; needs skip rule

Part C: Full Hand Computation; Mirrors Assignment 2 Q2

EXAM ALERT

This is the exact type of problem from Assignment 2, Question 2. You will be given a quadratic function with $H_0 = I$ and exact line search, and asked to do SR1 iterations. The key result: SR1 can recover $Q^{-1}$ in as few as ONE iteration for the right problem.

SR1 on $f(x) = \frac{1}{2}x^T Q x$ with $Q = \begin{bmatrix} 2 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix}$, $x_0 = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix}$, $H_0 = I$

Note: $\nabla f(x) = Qx$. Minimum at $x^* = [0, 0]^T$. True inverse Hessian: $Q^{-1} = \begin{bmatrix} 1/2 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix}$.

ITERATION 0 ($k = 0$)

Step 1: Gradient at $x_0$

$$g_0 = Qx_0 = \begin{bmatrix} 2 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix}\begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 2 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix}$$

Step 2: Search direction

$$p_0 = -H_0 g_0 = -I\begin{bmatrix} 2 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} -2 \\ -1 \end{bmatrix}$$

Since $H_0 = I$, this is just steepest descent.

Step 3: Exact line search for $\alpha_0$

$$\alpha_0 = -\frac{g_0^T p_0}{p_0^T Q p_0}$$

Numerator:

$$g_0^T p_0 = (2)(-2) + (1)(-1) = -4 - 1 = -5$$

Denominator: First compute $Qp_0$:

$$Qp_0 = \begin{bmatrix} 2 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix}\begin{bmatrix} -2 \\ -1 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} -4 \\ -1 \end{bmatrix}$$ $$p_0^T Q p_0 = (-2)(-4) + (-1)(-1) = 8 + 1 = 9$$ $$\alpha_0 = -\frac{-5}{9} = \frac{5}{9}$$

Step 4: New point $x_1$

$$x_1 = x_0 + \alpha_0 p_0 = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix} + \frac{5}{9}\begin{bmatrix} -2 \\ -1 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 1 - 10/9 \\ 1 - 5/9 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} -1/9 \\ 4/9 \end{bmatrix}$$

Step 5: Gradient at $x_1$

$$g_1 = Qx_1 = \begin{bmatrix} 2 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix}\begin{bmatrix} -1/9 \\ 4/9 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} -2/9 \\ 4/9 \end{bmatrix}$$

Step 6: Compute $s_0$ and $y_0$

$$s_0 = x_1 - x_0 = \begin{bmatrix} -1/9 - 1 \\ 4/9 - 1 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} -10/9 \\ -5/9 \end{bmatrix}$$ $$y_0 = g_1 - g_0 = \begin{bmatrix} -2/9 - 2 \\ 4/9 - 1 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} -20/9 \\ -5/9 \end{bmatrix}$$

Sanity check: $s_0 = \alpha_0 p_0 = \frac{5}{9}[-2, -1]^T = [-10/9, -5/9]^T$ ✓

Step 7: Compute the prediction error $s_0 - H_0 y_0$

Since $H_0 = I$:

$$H_0 y_0 = I \begin{bmatrix} -20/9 \\ -5/9 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} -20/9 \\ -5/9 \end{bmatrix}$$ $$s_0 - H_0 y_0 = \begin{bmatrix} -10/9 \\ -5/9 \end{bmatrix} - \begin{bmatrix} -20/9 \\ -5/9 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 10/9 \\ 0 \end{bmatrix}$$

Interpretation: The prediction error is entirely in the $x_1$-direction. The current map ($H_0 = I$) already predicts the $x_2$-component correctly but is wrong about $x_1$. SR1 will fix the $x_1$-component.

Step 8: Compute the denominator $(s_0 - H_0 y_0)^T y_0$

$$(s_0 - H_0 y_0)^T y_0 = \begin{bmatrix} 10/9 \\ 0 \end{bmatrix}^T \begin{bmatrix} -20/9 \\ -5/9 \end{bmatrix} = \left(\frac{10}{9}\right)\left(\frac{-20}{9}\right) + (0)\left(\frac{-5}{9}\right) = \frac{-200}{81}$$

This is non-zero, so the SR1 update is safe (no safeguard needed).

Step 9: Compute the outer product $(s_0 - H_0 y_0)(s_0 - H_0 y_0)^T$

$$\begin{bmatrix} 10/9 \\ 0 \end{bmatrix}\begin{bmatrix} 10/9 & 0 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 100/81 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 \end{bmatrix}$$

Step 10: Apply the SR1 update

$$H_1 = H_0 + \frac{(s_0 - H_0 y_0)(s_0 - H_0 y_0)^T}{(s_0 - H_0 y_0)^T y_0}$$ $$= \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix} + \frac{\begin{bmatrix} 100/81 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 \end{bmatrix}}{-200/81}$$ $$= \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix} + \begin{bmatrix} (100/81) \div (-200/81) & 0 \\ 0 & 0 \end{bmatrix}$$ $$= \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix} + \begin{bmatrix} -1/2 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 \end{bmatrix}$$

$$\boxed{H_1 = \begin{bmatrix} 1/2 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix} = Q^{-1} \quad \text{EXACTLY!}}$$

After just ONE SR1 update, we have recovered the exact inverse Hessian! This happened because the prediction error was entirely in one direction ($x_1$), and the rank-1 correction fixed that one direction perfectly. The $x_2$-direction was already correct in $H_0 = I$ (since $Q^{-1}$ has a 1 in the $(2,2)$ entry).

ITERATION 1 ($k = 1$); Confirmation

Since $H_1 = Q^{-1}$, the next step is a Newton step and converges exactly.

Step 1: Search direction

$$p_1 = -H_1 g_1 = -\begin{bmatrix} 1/2 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix}\begin{bmatrix} -2/9 \\ 4/9 \end{bmatrix} = -\begin{bmatrix} -1/9 \\ 4/9 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 1/9 \\ -4/9 \end{bmatrix}$$

Step 2: Exact line search for $\alpha_1$

$$g_1^T p_1 = \left(-\frac{2}{9}\right)\left(\frac{1}{9}\right) + \left(\frac{4}{9}\right)\left(-\frac{4}{9}\right) = \frac{-2}{81} + \frac{-16}{81} = \frac{-18}{81} = \frac{-2}{9}$$

Compute $Qp_1$:

$$Qp_1 = \begin{bmatrix} 2 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix}\begin{bmatrix} 1/9 \\ -4/9 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 2/9 \\ -4/9 \end{bmatrix}$$ $$p_1^T Q p_1 = \left(\frac{1}{9}\right)\left(\frac{2}{9}\right) + \left(-\frac{4}{9}\right)\left(-\frac{4}{9}\right) = \frac{2}{81} + \frac{16}{81} = \frac{18}{81} = \frac{2}{9}$$ $$\alpha_1 = -\frac{-2/9}{2/9} = 1$$

Step 3: New point

$$x_2 = x_1 + \alpha_1 p_1 = \begin{bmatrix} -1/9 \\ 4/9 \end{bmatrix} + 1 \cdot \begin{bmatrix} 1/9 \\ -4/9 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 0 \\ 0 \end{bmatrix} = x^*$$

Converged to the exact minimum in 2 total iterations! ✓

Notice that $\alpha_1 = 1$ , the full Newton step. When $H_k = Q^{-1}$ exactly, the quasi-Newton direction equals the Newton direction, and the exact line search gives $\alpha = 1$. This is the hallmark of a perfect inverse Hessian approximation.

Why Did SR1 Recover $Q^{-1}$ in One Step?

For this particular problem, $Q$ is diagonal with entries 2 and 1. The identity $H_0 = I$ already has the correct $(2,2)$ entry ($Q^{-1}_{22} = 1/1 = 1 = I_{22}$). Only the $(1,1)$ entry is wrong ($Q^{-1}_{11} = 1/2 \neq 1 = I_{11}$). Since the error is rank 1 (only one entry needs fixing), a single rank-1 SR1 update can fix it exactly.

In general, if $H_0$ differs from $Q^{-1}$ by a matrix of rank $r$, then SR1 needs exactly $r$ iterations to recover $Q^{-1}$ (on a quadratic with exact line search). BFGS, with its rank-2 updates, needs at most $\lceil r/2 \rceil$ iterations; but the situation is more nuanced in practice.

Part D: DFP Method

Physical analogy; BFGS's older sibling.

DFP (Davidon-Fletcher-Powell) was the FIRST quasi-Newton method, published in 1959 by William Davidon and refined by Fletcher and Powell. It predates BFGS by about a decade. Think of DFP as the prototype and BFGS as the improved version. They share the same overall idea (update an inverse Hessian approximation using $s_k$ and $y_k$), but BFGS turns out to be more numerically stable in practice.
DFP inverse Hessian update: $$H_{k+1} = H_k - \frac{H_k y_k y_k^T H_k}{y_k^T H_k y_k} + \frac{s_k s_k^T}{s_k^T y_k}$$
$H_k$
current inverse Hessian approximation
$y_k = g_{k+1} - g_k$
gradient change
$s_k = x_{k+1} - x_k$
step taken
$H_k y_k$
matrix-vector product: $H_k$ times the gradient change
$H_k y_k y_k^T H_k$
outer product involving $H_k y_k$; a rank-1 matrix
$y_k^T H_k y_k$
a scalar: the gradient change's "energy" under the current inverse Hessian
$s_k s_k^T$
outer product of the step with itself; rank-1 matrix
$s_k^T y_k$
a scalar: the curvature condition value (must be $\gt 0$ for the update to be valid)

Like BFGS, this is a rank-2 update: subtract one rank-1 matrix ($\frac{H_k y_k y_k^T H_k}{y_k^T H_k y_k}$) and add another ($\frac{s_k s_k^T}{s_k^T y_k}$).

DFP is the "Dual" of BFGS

There is a beautiful mathematical relationship between DFP and BFGS:

  • BFGS updates $H_k$ (inverse Hessian) using $s_k$ and $y_k$ in a certain pattern.
  • DFP updates $H_k$ using the SAME formula structure, but with the roles of $s$ and $y$ swapped in a specific way.

Equivalently:

  • If you apply the BFGS formula to update the direct Hessian $B_k$ and then invert, you get the DFP formula for $H_k$.
  • If you apply the DFP formula to update $B_k$ and then invert, you get the BFGS formula for $H_k$.

They are "complementary" or "dual" methods. On a quadratic with exact line search, they produce the same sequence of iterates. On general functions, they differ; and BFGS is consistently better.

Why BFGS is preferred over DFP:
  • Self-correcting behavior: BFGS has better self-correcting properties. If $H_k$ has accumulated errors, BFGS tends to fix them faster than DFP.
  • Numerical stability: DFP can become ill-conditioned more easily in the presence of round-off errors.
  • Empirical evidence: Decades of computational experiments show BFGS outperforms DFP on almost all practical problems.
  • Scale sensitivity: DFP is more sensitive to the scaling of the objective function.

Glossary

SR1 (Symmetric Rank-One)
A quasi-Newton update that adds a rank-1 correction to the Hessian approximation. Simpler than BFGS but does not preserve positive definiteness.
DFP (Davidon-Fletcher-Powell)
The first quasi-Newton method (1959). A rank-2 update that is the "dual" of BFGS. Preserves PD but is less numerically stable than BFGS.
Rank of an update
The maximum number of independent "directions" the correction matrix can change. Rank 1 = one direction changed. Rank 2 = two directions changed.
Prediction error ($s_k - H_k y_k$)
How wrong the current inverse Hessian approximation was in predicting the step from the gradient change. If zero, the approximation is already correct on that step.
Safeguard (skip rule)
A check that prevents the SR1 update when the denominator is too small. Skips the update and keeps $H_{k+1} = H_k$.
Positive definiteness (PD)
A matrix property meaning "bowl-shaped" (all eigenvalues positive). BFGS and DFP preserve this; SR1 does not. See Page 03 for full explanation.
Trust region
A framework that bounds the step size by a radius $\Delta_k$, making it safe to use non-PD Hessian approximations. See Page 09.
Dual methods
BFGS and DFP are duals: swap the roles of $s_k$ and $y_k$ (and $B_k$ and $H_k$) in one to get the other.
Secant condition
$H_{k+1} y_k = s_k$ (inverse form) or $B_{k+1} s_k = y_k$ (direct form). All quasi-Newton methods satisfy this. See Page 12.
