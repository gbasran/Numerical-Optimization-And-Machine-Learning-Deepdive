Page 01: Taylor's Theorem, Norms & Convergence Rates

The mathematical toolkit that every optimization method is built on; explained from scratch with physical analogies

Part A: Taylor's Theorem; Predicting Functions from Local Information

CORE IDEA
Analogy: GPS prediction. Imagine you are driving down the highway and your GPS is trying to predict where you will be in 10 seconds. With zero information beyond your current location, the GPS would just say "you are right here", that is, the zeroth-order approximation. Now add your speed: the GPS says "you are here, moving north at 100 km/h, so in 10 seconds you will be about 278 meters north." That is the first-order approximation; it uses your current position plus your rate of change (velocity). But what if you are accelerating onto a highway ramp? The GPS should also factor in your acceleration: "you are here, moving at 100 km/h, accelerating at 5 m/s², so in 10 seconds you will be even farther than the straight-line estimate." That is the second-order approximation.

Taylor's theorem does exactly the same thing for mathematical functions. It approximates a complicated function near a known point using local information: the function value (position), its derivative (speed), and its second derivative (acceleration). The more terms you include, the better your prediction; but even the first few terms are remarkably powerful, and they are the entire engine behind gradient descent, Newton's method, and trust regions.

First-order Taylor expansion (linear approximation):

$$f(x + p) \approx f(x) + \nabla f(x)^T p$$
$f$
The function we are trying to approximate; for example, the cost or loss we want to minimize.
$x$
The current point; where we are standing right now (our "GPS location").
$p$
The step we are about to take; a direction and distance from $x$.
$\nabla f(x)$
The gradient (vector of partial derivatives) at $x$; this is the "velocity" of the function. It tells us the slope in every direction. See Page 02 for a full explanation of the gradient.
$\nabla f(x)^T p$
The dot product of the gradient with the step. This gives a single number: the predicted change in $f$ when we move by $p$. Positive means $f$ goes up; negative means $f$ goes down.

Second-order Taylor expansion (quadratic approximation):

$$f(x + p) \approx f(x) + \nabla f(x)^T p + \frac{1}{2} p^T \nabla^2 f(x)\, p$$
$\nabla^2 f(x)$
The Hessian matrix; a square matrix of second derivatives. This is the "acceleration" of the function. It captures curvature: how fast the slope itself is changing. See Page 02, Part C for the full treatment.
$\frac{1}{2} p^T \nabla^2 f(x)\, p$
A quadratic form; it bends the linear approximation into a parabola (or bowl in multiple dimensions). This correction accounts for curvature, making the approximation much more accurate.

Why Does This Matter for This Course?

Every single optimization method you will encounter uses some form of Taylor approximation:

  • Gradient descent (Pages 04–05) uses the first-order approximation. It only knows the slope, so it picks the steepest-downhill direction.
  • Newton's method (Page 07) uses the second-order approximation. It builds a local bowl (quadratic model) and jumps to its bottom in one step.
  • Trust regions (Page 09) also use the second-order approximation, but they add a "safety leash" that limits step size.
  • Quasi-Newton / BFGS (Page 12) approximates the Hessian from gradient history, then uses the second-order expansion.

If you understand Taylor's theorem, you understand why each method works.

The quadratic (2nd-order) hugs the true curve much more closely near $x_0$.

Key takeaway: Taylor approximation trades global accuracy for local simplicity. Near $x_0$ the approximation is excellent. Far away it breaks down. This is why line searches and trust regions exist; they make sure we don't step so far that our Taylor model becomes garbage.

Hand Computation: Taylor Approximation of $f(x) = e^x$ at $x_0 = 0$

This is a single-variable example to build intuition before we hit multiple dimensions.

Setup:
$f(x) = e^x$, expand around $x_0 = 0$. The step is $p = x - x_0 = x$.
Step 1: Zeroth-order (just the function value).
$f(0) = e^0 = 1$
Approximation: $f(x) \approx 1$. (A horizontal line. Terrible prediction away from 0.)
Step 2: First-order (add the derivative).
$f'(x) = e^x$, so $f'(0) = 1$.
Approximation: $f(x) \approx 1 + 1 \cdot x = 1 + x$. (A straight line through $(0,1)$ with slope 1.)
Step 3: Second-order (add curvature).
$f''(x) = e^x$, so $f''(0) = 1$.
Approximation: $f(x) \approx 1 + x + \frac{1}{2}(1)x^2 = 1 + x + \frac{x^2}{2}$. (A parabola; much closer to $e^x$.)
Step 4: Test the predictions at $x = 0.5$.
True value: $e^{0.5} = 1.6487$
Zeroth-order: $1$.   Error: $0.6487$ (off by 39%)
First-order: $1.5$.   Error: $0.1487$ (off by 9%)
Second-order: $1.625$.   Error: $0.0237$ (off by 1.4%)

Each additional term dramatically improves accuracy near $x_0$.

This is the fundamental pattern: more Taylor terms = more information used = better local prediction. In optimization, the first-order term tells us the slope (gradient), and the second-order term tells us the curvature (Hessian).

Connecting to multiple dimensions: In this course, $x$ is usually a vector (like $[x_1, x_2]^T$) and $p$ is a step direction. The first-order term $\nabla f(x)^T p$ replaces $f'(x_0) \cdot p$, and the second-order term $\frac{1}{2}p^T \nabla^2 f(x) \, p$ replaces $\frac{1}{2}f''(x_0) \cdot p^2$. Same idea, more dimensions. See Page 02 for the full multivariable treatment.

Part B: Norms; Measuring Distance and Size

MEASURING TOOL
Analogy: Measuring a road trip. Suppose you need to get from point A to point B in a city. There are different ways to measure how "far" you have to go:

L1 norm (Manhattan / taxi-cab distance): This is the total distance you actually drive on a grid of city streets; you can only go north/south or east/west. If you drive 3 blocks east and 4 blocks north, your L1 distance is $3 + 4 = 7$ blocks.

L2 norm (Euclidean / bird-fly distance): This is the straight-line distance a bird would fly diagonally from A to B. For 3 blocks east and 4 blocks north, the bird flies $\sqrt{3^2 + 4^2} = 5$ blocks.

L-infinity norm (Chebyshev distance): This is the longest single stretch in any one direction. Among the 3 blocks east and 4 blocks north, the biggest is 4. So $\|x\|_\infty = 4$. Think of it as "the bottleneck dimension"; the direction in which you have the farthest to go.

L1 norm (Manhattan):

$$\|x\|_1 = |x_1| + |x_2| + \cdots + |x_n|$$

Sum of absolute values of all components.

L2 norm (Euclidean):

$$\|x\|_2 = \sqrt{x_1^2 + x_2^2 + \cdots + x_n^2}$$

Square root of sum of squared components. This is the "default" norm and the one used most often in this course.

L-infinity norm (Max):

$$\|x\|_\infty = \max(|x_1|, |x_2|, \ldots, |x_n|)$$

The single largest absolute component.

Unit balls: all points where $\|x\| = 1$ for each norm.
The diamond (L1) has corners on the axes. The circle (L2) is round. The square (L-infinity) has flat sides.

Hand Computation: All Three Norms of $\mathbf{x} = [3, -4]^T$

L1 norm:
$\|x\|_1 = |3| + |-4| = 3 + 4 = $ 7
L2 norm:
$\|x\|_2 = \sqrt{3^2 + (-4)^2} = \sqrt{9 + 16} = \sqrt{25} = $ 5
L-infinity norm:
$\|x\|_\infty = \max(|3|, |-4|) = \max(3, 4) = $ 4

Notice: L2 is always between L-infinity and L1. Here $4 \leq 5 \leq 7$. This is always true.

Where norms appear in this course: Stopping criteria use $\|\nabla f(x_k)\| \lt \epsilon$ (we stop when the gradient is "small enough"). Convergence rates measure $\|x_k - x^*\|$ (distance from the current guess to the true answer). Trust regions limit $\|p\| \leq \Delta$ (the step must stay within a ball of radius $\Delta$).

Part C: Convergence Rates; How Fast Do We Get There?

SPEED OF ALGORITHMS
Analogy: Paying off debt.

Q-linear convergence is like paying a fixed percentage of your debt each month. Say your debt starts at 1000 and you pay off 50% each month. It goes: 1000, 500, 250, 125, 62.50, ... Every month you cut the debt in half, but you never wipe it out in one fell swoop. The ratio of consecutive debts is always 0.5 (or whatever fixed rate $r$). This is decent, but predictable.

Q-superlinear convergence is like your monthly payment rate accelerating over time. First month you pay 50%, next month 60%, then 75%, then 90%, then 99%... The ratio of consecutive errors shrinks toward zero. Each step is proportionally bigger than the last. Quasi-Newton methods (like BFGS) achieve this.

Q-quadratic convergence is like the debt squaring away. The number of correct digits doubles each step: 1 correct digit, then 2, then 4, then 8, then 16. After just 5 steps you have 16-digit accuracy. Newton's method achieves this (when it converges). It is spectacularly fast.

Q-linear convergence:

$$\|x_{k+1} - x^*\| \leq r\, \|x_k - x^*\| \quad \text{for some } r \in (0, 1)$$
$x_k$
The approximation at step $k$.
$x^*$
The true solution we are converging to.
$\|x_k - x^*\|$
The error at step $k$; how far we are from the answer.
$r$
The convergence ratio; a fixed number strictly between 0 and 1. Smaller $r$ means faster convergence.

The error shrinks by at least a constant factor each step. Steepest descent achieves Q-linear convergence.

Q-superlinear convergence:

$$\lim_{k \to \infty} \frac{\|x_{k+1} - x^*\|}{\|x_k - x^*\|} = 0$$
Ratio $\to 0$
The ratio of consecutive errors is not stuck at a fixed $r$; it shrinks toward zero. Each step is proportionally more effective than the last.

Faster than linear, but the ratio decays rather than the error squaring. BFGS achieves this.

Q-quadratic convergence:

$$\|x_{k+1} - x^*\| \leq M\, \|x_k - x^*\|^2$$
$M$
A positive constant (does not depend on $k$).
$\|x_k - x^*\|^2$
The error squared. If the error is 0.01, the next error is at most $M \cdot 0.0001$. The number of correct digits roughly doubles each step.

Newton's method achieves Q-quadratic convergence near the solution (when the Hessian is nonsingular).

Hand Computation (Quiz 1 style): Convergence of $x_k = 1/k!$

The sequence is $x_1 = 1, \; x_2 = 1/2, \; x_3 = 1/6, \; x_4 = 1/24, \; x_5 = 1/120, \; x_6 = 1/720, \ldots$ with $x^* = 0$.

Step 1: Write the error.
$e_k = |x_k - x^*| = |1/k! - 0| = 1/k!$
Step 2: Compute the ratio $e_{k+1}/e_k$.
$\displaystyle\frac{e_{k+1}}{e_k} = \frac{1/(k+1)!}{1/k!} = \frac{k!}{(k+1)!} = \frac{1}{k+1}$
Step 3: Check for Q-linear.
For Q-linear convergence we need $e_{k+1}/e_k \leq r$ for some fixed $r \lt 1$.
Since $1/(k+1)$ changes with $k$ (it is not a fixed constant), this is not merely Q-linear; it is at least superlinear.
(Technically we could pick $r$ to be the largest ratio, which is $1/2$ at $k=1$, so it is Q-linear too. But the important point is it does better than linear.)
Step 4: Check for Q-superlinear.
$\displaystyle\lim_{k \to \infty} \frac{e_{k+1}}{e_k} = \lim_{k \to \infty} \frac{1}{k+1} = 0$
The limit is 0. This is Q-superlinear convergence.
Step 5: Check for Q-quadratic.
$\displaystyle\frac{e_{k+1}}{e_k^2} = \frac{1/(k+1)!}{(1/k!)^2} = \frac{(k!)^2}{(k+1)!} = \frac{(k!)^2}{(k+1) \cdot k!} = \frac{k!}{k+1}$
As $k \to \infty$, $k!/(k+1) \to \infty$, so this ratio blows up.
NOT Q-quadratic; the error does not shrink as fast as the square of the previous error.

Final answer: The sequence $x_k = 1/k!$ converges Q-superlinearly to $0$, but not Q-quadratically.

Part D: Condition Numbers; Why Some Problems Are Harder

PROBLEM DIFFICULTY
Analogy: Bathtub vs. round bowl. Imagine you drop a marble into a perfectly round bowl. The marble rolls straight to the bottom without zigzagging; the bowl is symmetric in every direction, so the steepest-downhill direction always points straight at the center. This bowl has condition number $\kappa = 1$.

Now imagine you drop a marble into an elongated bathtub; the shape of a hot dog. The sides are very steep but the bottom is a long, almost flat valley. The marble does NOT roll straight to the lowest point. Instead it bounces back and forth between the steep sides, making tiny progress along the flat bottom each time. It zigzags, wasting enormous effort. This bathtub has a large condition number.

This is exactly what happens to gradient descent on ill-conditioned problems. The gradient (steepest downhill) keeps pointing toward the steep sides, not along the valley floor. The method zigzags back and forth, converging painfully slowly. This is not a bug in gradient descent; it is a fundamental property of the problem's geometry.

Condition number of a symmetric positive definite matrix $A$:

$$\kappa(A) = \frac{\lambda_{\max}}{\lambda_{\min}}$$
$\lambda_{\max}$
The largest eigenvalue of $A$; represents the steepest curvature direction.
$\lambda_{\min}$
The smallest eigenvalue of $A$; represents the flattest curvature direction.
$\kappa(A)$
The ratio. When $\kappa = 1$, all directions have equal curvature (round bowl). When $\kappa$ is large, the landscape is elongated (bathtub).

Why Does the Condition Number Control Convergence Speed?

For steepest descent on a quadratic, the convergence factor per step is:

$$r = \frac{\kappa - 1}{\kappa + 1}$$

When $\kappa = 1$: $r = 0/(2) = 0$ , instant convergence in one step (round bowl).

When $\kappa = 100$: $r = 99/101 \approx 0.98$; each step only reduces the error by about 2%. It takes hundreds of steps.

When $\kappa = 1000$: $r = 999/1001 \approx 0.998$; each step reduces error by 0.2%. Thousands of steps needed.

This is why Newton's method and BFGS exist; they effectively transform the problem so that it "looks" more like a round bowl, dramatically improving the convergence.

Glossary

Key Terms from This Page

Taylor expansion
A way to approximate a function near a known point using the function's value, first derivative (gradient), second derivative (Hessian), and higher-order derivatives. The more terms you include, the better the approximation locally.
First-order approximation
Uses only the function value and gradient: $f(x+p) \approx f(x) + \nabla f(x)^T p$. This gives a linear (flat plane) approximation. Gradient descent is based on this.
Second-order approximation
Adds the Hessian term: $f(x+p) \approx f(x) + \nabla f(x)^T p + \frac{1}{2}p^T \nabla^2 f(x) p$. This gives a quadratic (bowl-shaped) approximation. Newton's method is based on this.
Norm
A function that measures the "size" or "length" of a vector. Must satisfy: (1) $\|x\| \geq 0$ with equality only when $x = 0$, (2) $\|cx\| = |c| \|x\|$ (scaling), (3) $\|x + y\| \leq \|x\| + \|y\|$ (triangle inequality).
L1 norm ($\|\cdot\|_1$)
Sum of absolute values: $|x_1| + |x_2| + \cdots + |x_n|$. Also called the Manhattan or taxicab norm. Its unit ball is a diamond shape.
L2 norm ($\|\cdot\|_2$)
Euclidean length: $\sqrt{x_1^2 + x_2^2 + \cdots + x_n^2}$. The "default" norm. Its unit ball is a circle (in 2D) or sphere (in 3D).
L-infinity norm ($\|\cdot\|_\infty$)
Maximum absolute component: $\max(|x_1|, |x_2|, \ldots, |x_n|)$. Also called the Chebyshev norm. Its unit ball is a square (in 2D) or cube (in 3D).
Convergence rate
A measure of how quickly an iterative method approaches the true solution. Faster convergence means fewer iterations (and less computation) to reach a given accuracy.
Q-linear convergence
The error decreases by at least a constant factor each step: $e_{k+1} \leq r \cdot e_k$ for some fixed $r \in (0,1)$. Steepest descent achieves this. "Q" stands for "quotient" (we look at the ratio of consecutive errors).
Q-superlinear convergence
The ratio of consecutive errors $e_{k+1}/e_k$ shrinks to zero. Each step becomes proportionally more effective. BFGS and secant methods achieve this. Faster than Q-linear, slower than Q-quadratic.
Q-quadratic convergence
The error satisfies $e_{k+1} \leq M \cdot e_k^2$. The number of correct digits roughly doubles each step. Newton's method achieves this near the solution. Spectacularly fast once you are close.
R-convergence
A weaker notion of convergence rate that looks at the overall sequence behavior rather than step-to-step ratios. A sequence has R-linear convergence of order $r$ if $e_k \leq C \cdot r^k$ for some constant $C$. The error is bounded by a geometric sequence, even if individual ratios fluctuate.
Condition number ($\kappa$)
The ratio $\lambda_{\max}/\lambda_{\min}$ of the largest to smallest eigenvalue of a positive definite matrix. Measures how "elongated" the problem's geometry is. $\kappa = 1$ means perfectly round (easy). Large $\kappa$ means badly stretched (hard for gradient descent). Gradient descent's convergence factor is $(\kappa - 1)/(\kappa + 1)$.
Error sequence
The sequence of distances $e_k = \|x_k - x^*\|$ from each iterate to the true solution. Analyzing this sequence (ratios, limits, bounds) is how we determine the convergence rate of an algorithm.
