Nonlinear Conjugate Gradient

Extending the "never revisit directions" trick from linear systems to general optimization; the simplest fast method

Part A: From Linear to Nonlinear CG

Physical analogy; upgrading your hiking strategy for rougher terrain.

On Page 10, we learned Linear CG: a brilliant hiking strategy for perfectly bowl-shaped valleys ($Ax = b$, which is the same as minimizing $f(x) = \frac{1}{2}x^TAx - b^Tx$). The key insight was: pick smart, "conjugate" directions so you never have to revisit a direction you already explored. In a bowl-shaped valley with $n$ dimensions, this guarantees you reach the bottom in at most $n$ steps.

But real optimization problems are NOT perfect bowls. The terrain is bumpy, twisted, has ridges and saddle points. Nonlinear CG takes the same "never revisit" philosophy and adapts it for this rough terrain. The result: a method that is almost as simple as gradient descent (only uses gradients, no Hessians) but converges much faster.

Here are the three key changes when going from linear CG to nonlinear CG:

Change 1: Residual $\to$ Gradient

In linear CG, we used the residual $r_k = b - Ax_k$ to measure "how far we are from solving $Ax = b$." For a general function $f(x)$, there is no matrix $A$ or vector $b$. Instead, we use the gradient $g_k = \nabla f(x_k)$ (recall from Page 02).

This makes sense: for the quadratic $f(x) = \frac{1}{2}x^TAx - b^Tx$, the gradient is $\nabla f(x) = Ax - b = -r_k$. So the gradient is just the negative residual; same information, opposite sign.
$r_k = b - Ax_k$
linear CG residual; only works for $Ax = b$
$g_k = \nabla f(x_k)$
nonlinear CG gradient; works for ANY differentiable $f$
Change 2: Exact step size $\to$ Line search

In linear CG, we had an exact formula for the step size: $\alpha_k = \frac{r_k^T r_k}{p_k^T A p_k}$. This formula depended on knowing $A$. For a general function, we dont have $A$, so we use a line search to find $\alpha_k$ (recall from Page 08). We typically use the strong Wolfe conditions to ensure the step is good enough.
Change 3: New $\beta$ formulas

In linear CG, the formula for $\beta_k$ was $\beta_k = \frac{r_{k+1}^T r_{k+1}}{r_k^T r_k}$. For nonlinear problems, there are SEVERAL different formulas for $\beta_k$, and they lead to different methods (Fletcher-Reeves, Polak-Ribiere, etc.). On a quadratic, they all reduce to the same thing, but on general functions, they behave very differently.

Why Nonlinear CG?

Nonlinear CG extends the "no revisiting" property of linear CG to general functions. It is popular because:

  • Simple: only uses gradients (like gradient descent); no Hessians, no matrix storage
  • Memory efficient: $O(n)$ storage; just a few vectors
  • Much faster than GD: avoids the zigzag problem of gradient descent
  • Scales to large problems: because it doesnt store or update any $n \times n$ matrix (unlike BFGS on Page 12)

Part B: Fletcher-Reeves (FR) Method

Physical analogy; energy ratio.

Imagine you are hiking and tracking your "gradient energy"; how steep the hill is beneath your feet. Fletcher-Reeves says: compare your current energy to your previous energy. If the terrain got steeper (current energy is larger), keep more of your old hiking direction mixed in; you are in rough territory and the old direction was helping. If the terrain got gentler (current energy is smaller), rely more on the new steepest-descent direction; you are getting close and should trust the local slope.
Fletcher-Reeves $\beta$ formula: $$\beta_{k+1}^{FR} = \frac{\|\nabla f(x_{k+1})\|^2}{\|\nabla f(x_k)\|^2} = \frac{g_{k+1}^T g_{k+1}}{g_k^T g_k}$$
$g_k = \nabla f(x_k)$
gradient at the current point
$g_{k+1} = \nabla f(x_{k+1})$
gradient at the new point (after taking a step)
$\|g\|^2 = g^T g$
squared norm of the gradient; the "gradient energy" (recall norms from Page 01)
$\beta_{k+1}^{FR}$
the mixing coefficient; how much of the old direction $p_k$ to blend into the new direction $p_{k+1}$

The ratio $\frac{\|g_{k+1}\|^2}{\|g_k\|^2}$ is always non-negative (both numerator and denominator are squared norms). If the gradient got bigger, $\beta > 1$. If the gradient got smaller, $\beta < 1$. If the gradient stayed the same size, $\beta = 1$.

Here is the full Fletcher-Reeves algorithm, line by line:

Algorithm: Fletcher-Reeves Nonlinear CG
Input: Starting point $x_0$, function $f$, gradient $\nabla f$, tolerance $\epsilon$
Step 0: $g_0 = \nabla f(x_0)$
  Compute the gradient at the starting point.
Step 1: $p_0 = -g_0$
  First direction = steepest descent. We have no history yet, so just go downhill.
For $k = 0, 1, 2, \ldots$ repeat:
Step 2: Find $\alpha_k$ via line search satisfying the strong Wolfe conditions (Page 08)
  How far to walk in direction $p_k$. The Wolfe conditions ensure the step is "good enough."
Step 3: $x_{k+1} = x_k + \alpha_k p_k$
  Take the step: move from current point along direction $p_k$ by amount $\alpha_k$.
Step 4: $g_{k+1} = \nabla f(x_{k+1})$
  Compute gradient at new point.
Step 5: If $\|g_{k+1}\| < \epsilon$: STOP; we have converged.
  Gradient is nearly zero = nearly at a stationary point.
Step 6: $\beta_{k+1}^{FR} = \dfrac{g_{k+1}^T g_{k+1}}{g_k^T g_k}$
  The Fletcher-Reeves mixing coefficient: ratio of new gradient energy to old gradient energy.
Step 7: $p_{k+1} = -g_{k+1} + \beta_{k+1}^{FR} \, p_k$
  New direction = (steepest descent at new point) + (a fraction of the old direction).
Step 8 (Optional restart): Every $n$ steps, set $p_k = -g_k$
  Reset to steepest descent to refresh the conjugacy. See below for why.

Why Restart?

On a perfectly quadratic function, conjugacy is maintained automatically; the directions stay perfectly independent. But on a general nonlinear function, the conjugacy property degrades over many iterations. The directions start to lose their independence, and the method can stall or even diverge.

Restarting every $n$ steps (where $n$ is the number of variables) refreshes the conjugacy. Its like recalibrating a compass: after traveling through magnetic terrain for a while, you stop and check your bearings from scratch.

In practice, the restart rule is: if $k$ is a multiple of $n$, set $p_k = -g_k$ (pure steepest descent) and continue building conjugate directions from there.

Part C: Polak-Ribière (PR) and PR+

Physical analogy; terrain change detector.

Fletcher-Reeves asks: "How steep is it now vs. before?" Polak-Ribière asks a more refined question: "How much has the slope CHANGED?" Imagine you are hiking and you have two slope measurements; one from your current position and one from where you were. If the slope changed a lot (you crossed a ridge, entered a valley, or the terrain shifted dramatically), the old direction is probably not useful anymore; time for a fresh start. If the slope barely changed (smooth, predictable terrain), keep blending the old direction.
Polak-Ribière $\beta$ formula: $$\beta_{k+1}^{PR} = \frac{g_{k+1}^T(g_{k+1} - g_k)}{g_k^T g_k}$$
$g_{k+1} - g_k$
the change in gradient from step $k$ to step $k+1$; measures how much the terrain shifted
$g_{k+1}^T(g_{k+1} - g_k)$
dot product of the new gradient with the gradient change; projects the change onto the current slope direction
$g_k^T g_k$
squared norm of the old gradient (same denominator as FR)
$\beta_{k+1}^{PR}$
the mixing coefficient; can be positive, negative, or zero

Lets expand the numerator to see the connection to FR:

Expanding the PR numerator: $$g_{k+1}^T(g_{k+1} - g_k) = g_{k+1}^T g_{k+1} - g_{k+1}^T g_k$$

Compare this with Fletcher-Reeves, which has numerator $g_{k+1}^T g_{k+1}$. The difference is the term $-g_{k+1}^T g_k$.

On a quadratic with exact line search, consecutive gradients are orthogonal: $g_{k+1}^T g_k = 0$. So the $-g_{k+1}^T g_k$ term vanishes, and PR reduces to FR. This is why both methods are identical on quadratics but different on general functions.

Why PR is Usually Better Than FR; Built-In Restart

Here is the key insight that makes PR superior in practice:

When $g_{k+1} \approx g_k$ (the gradient barely changed; you are near a smooth, nearly-quadratic region close to the minimum), the numerator $g_{k+1}^T(g_{k+1} - g_k) \approx 0$. So $\beta^{PR} \approx 0$, and the new direction becomes:

$$p_{k+1} = -g_{k+1} + 0 \cdot p_k = -g_{k+1}$$

Thats just pure steepest descent! PR automatically restarts itself when the conjugacy has degraded. FR does NOT have this property; its $\beta^{FR}$ depends only on gradient magnitudes, not gradient changes, so it can keep blending in a stale direction even when it shouldnt.

The PR+ Variant; Forcing Non-Negativity $$\beta_{k+1}^{PR+} = \max\!\left(\beta_{k+1}^{PR},\; 0\right)$$

Unlike FR (which is always $\geq 0$), the PR formula CAN produce negative values. A negative $\beta$ means the new direction $p_{k+1} = -g_{k+1} + \beta p_k$ adds a negative multiple of the old direction; partially reversing course. This can cause problems.

PR+ clamps $\beta$ to be at least zero. When PR would give a negative value, PR+ simply restarts with pure steepest descent ($\beta = 0$). This gives us:

  • The automatic restart property of PR (when $\beta \approx 0$)
  • Protection against bad directions (when $\beta < 0$)
  • Global convergence guarantees (which raw PR lacks)
Technical note: Fletcher-Reeves with strong Wolfe conditions has a global convergence proof. Plain Polak-Ribière does NOT have a global convergence guarantee. But PR+ (with the $\max(0, \cdot)$ clamp) recovers global convergence while keeping PR's superior practical behavior. This is why PR+ is the recommended default.

Part D: Comparison and When to Use

Property Fletcher-Reeves (FR) Polak-Ribière (PR) PR+
Formula $\frac{\|g_{k+1}\|^2}{\|g_k\|^2}$ $\frac{g_{k+1}^T(g_{k+1}-g_k)}{g_k^Tg_k}$ $\max(\beta^{PR}, 0)$
$\beta$ sign Always $\geq 0$ Can be negative Always $\geq 0$
Auto-restart? No; needs manual every-$n$-step restart Yes; $\beta \to 0$ near minimum Yes; same as PR, plus clamp
Global convergence Yes (with strong Wolfe) Not guaranteed Yes (with strong Wolfe)
Practical speed Good, but can stall Usually faster Best of both worlds
On quadratics All three are identical; they reduce to the linear CG formula from Page 10
Bottom line: In practice, PR+ is almost always the best choice. FR is mainly of theoretical interest. Plain PR works well in practice but lacks convergence guarantees.

Why CG Avoids the Zigzag

Gradient descent always moves perpendicular to the contour lines. On elongated ellipses, this causes a zigzag pattern because each step overshoots across the narrow dimension. CG remembers its previous direction and corrects for this. On a quadratic in $n$ dimensions, CG converges in at most $n$ steps (here, $n = 2$, so 2 steps). GD can take hundreds of steps on the same problem.

Part E: Worked Example; One Iteration of Fletcher-Reeves

Fletcher-Reeves on $f(x_1, x_2) = 2x_1^2 + x_2^2$

Starting point: $x_0 = [2, 1]^T$. We will do one full iteration.

Step 0: Compute the gradient at $x_0$

The gradient of $f(x_1, x_2) = 2x_1^2 + x_2^2$ is:

$$g_0 = \nabla f(x_0) = \begin{bmatrix} 4x_1 \\ 2x_2 \end{bmatrix}_{x_0 = [2,1]} = \begin{bmatrix} 8 \\ 2 \end{bmatrix}$$

Step 1: First direction = steepest descent

$$p_0 = -g_0 = \begin{bmatrix} -8 \\ -2 \end{bmatrix}$$

Step 2: Line search for $\alpha_0$

We can write $f$ as a quadratic: $f(x) = \frac{1}{2}x^TQx$ with $Q = \begin{bmatrix} 4 & 0 \\ 0 & 2 \end{bmatrix}$.

For a quadratic, exact line search gives: $\alpha_k = -\dfrac{g_k^T p_k}{p_k^T Q p_k}$

Numerator: $g_0^T p_0 = (8)(-8) + (2)(-2) = -64 - 4 = -68$

For the denominator, first compute $Qp_0$:

$$Qp_0 = \begin{bmatrix} 4 & 0 \\ 0 & 2 \end{bmatrix}\begin{bmatrix} -8 \\ -2 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} -32 \\ -4 \end{bmatrix}$$

Then: $p_0^T Q p_0 = (-8)(-32) + (-2)(-4) = 256 + 8 = 264$

$$\alpha_0 = \frac{-(-68)}{264} = \frac{68}{264} = \frac{17}{66}$$

Step 3: Take the step

$$x_1 = x_0 + \alpha_0 p_0 = \begin{bmatrix} 2 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix} + \frac{17}{66}\begin{bmatrix} -8 \\ -2 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 2 - \frac{136}{66} \\ 1 - \frac{34}{66} \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} \frac{132 - 136}{66} \\ \frac{66 - 34}{66} \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} -\frac{4}{66} \\ \frac{32}{66} \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} -\frac{2}{33} \\ \frac{16}{33} \end{bmatrix}$$

Step 4: Compute gradient at new point

$$g_1 = \nabla f(x_1) = \begin{bmatrix} 4(-2/33) \\ 2(16/33) \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} -8/33 \\ 32/33 \end{bmatrix}$$

Step 5: Compute $\beta_1^{FR}$

$$\|g_1\|^2 = g_1^T g_1 = \left(\frac{-8}{33}\right)^2 + \left(\frac{32}{33}\right)^2 = \frac{64}{1089} + \frac{1024}{1089} = \frac{1088}{1089}$$ $$\|g_0\|^2 = g_0^T g_0 = 8^2 + 2^2 = 64 + 4 = 68$$ $$\beta_1^{FR} = \frac{\|g_1\|^2}{\|g_0\|^2} = \frac{1088/1089}{68} = \frac{1088}{74052} = \frac{16}{1089} \approx 0.01469$$

The gradient shrank dramatically (from 68 to about 1.0), so $\beta$ is small; the new direction will be mostly steepest descent with just a tiny blend of the old direction. This makes sense: we made great progress on the first step.

Step 6: Compute new direction $p_1$

$$p_1 = -g_1 + \beta_1^{FR} p_0 = \begin{bmatrix} 8/33 \\ -32/33 \end{bmatrix} + \frac{16}{1089}\begin{bmatrix} -8 \\ -2 \end{bmatrix}$$ $$= \begin{bmatrix} 8/33 - 128/1089 \\ -32/33 - 32/1089 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 264/1089 - 128/1089 \\ -1056/1089 - 32/1089 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 136/1089 \\ -1088/1089 \end{bmatrix}$$

The new search direction is $p_1 = \begin{bmatrix} 136/1089 \\ -1088/1089 \end{bmatrix}$. This is mostly in the $-x_2$ direction (downhill in the shallow dimension), with a small positive $x_1$ correction; exactly the kind of smart, non-zigzag direction CG produces.

What would happen next: We would do a line search along $p_1$ to find $\alpha_1$, compute $x_2$, and since this is a 2D quadratic, CG would converge to the exact minimum $x^* = [0, 0]^T$ in this second step. Just 2 iterations total for a 2D problem, compared to the many zigzag steps gradient descent would need.

Glossary

Nonlinear CG
Extension of the linear conjugate gradient method to general (non-quadratic) functions. Uses gradient information and line search instead of the matrix $A$.
Fletcher-Reeves (FR)
The first nonlinear CG method (1964). Uses $\beta^{FR} = \|g_{k+1}\|^2 / \|g_k\|^2$; the ratio of gradient magnitudes squared.
Polak-Ribière (PR)
A nonlinear CG method (1969) that uses the gradient change: $\beta^{PR} = g_{k+1}^T(g_{k+1} - g_k) / (g_k^Tg_k)$. Has a built-in automatic restart property.
PR+
The practical variant $\beta^{PR+} = \max(\beta^{PR}, 0)$. Keeps PRs automatic restart while ensuring global convergence.
Restart
Periodically resetting the search direction to steepest descent ($p_k = -g_k$) to refresh the conjugacy, which degrades on nonlinear functions.
Conjugacy degradation
On non-quadratic functions, the directions produced by CG gradually lose their conjugacy property, causing the method to slow down. Restarts fix this.
Strong Wolfe conditions
Line search conditions from Page 08 that ensure the step is adequate. Required for FR convergence proofs.
