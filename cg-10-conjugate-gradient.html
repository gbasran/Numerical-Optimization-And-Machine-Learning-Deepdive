Conjugate Gradient Methods

Solve $n$-dimensional systems in exactly $n$ steps — no zigzag, no wasted work — mirrors Quiz 5 exactly

Part A: The Problem CG Solves — Why GD Isn't Good Enough

Physical analogy — Gradient descent zigzags because it has no memory.

Imagine you are hiking down into an elongated valley — shaped like a bathtub that is very long and very narrow. Gradient descent (Page 06) always walks in the direction of steepest downhill. On the first step, you go steeply down the narrow wall of the valley. But this overshoots past the center and you end up on the opposite wall. On the next step, steepest downhill points back across the valley again. You zigzag back and forth, each step nearly perpendicular to the last, making painfully slow progress along the valley floor toward the actual minimum.

Why does this happen? Because gradient descent has no memory. Each step is chosen independently, using only the current gradient. It does not remember what directions it has already explored. So it keeps re-exploring the same narrow cross-valley direction, undoing progress from the previous step.

Conjugate gradient is the smart hiker. CG remembers which directions it has already explored and deliberately chooses each new direction to never interfere with previous progress. The mathematical word for "never interfere" is conjugate. If direction $p_0$ makes progress along one axis of the valley and direction $p_1$ is conjugate to $p_0$, then moving along $p_1$ does NOT undo any of the progress made along $p_0$. The two directions work independently — like painting different walls of a room without smearing each other's work.

The payoff is stunning: for a problem with $n$ unknowns, CG finds the exact answer in at most $n$ steps. Gradient descent might take thousands. For the $2 \times 2$ systems on our quizzes, CG converges in exactly 2 iterations — always.
The Problem: Solving $Ax = b$

CG solves the linear system $Ax = b$ where $A$ is a symmetric positive definite (SPD) matrix.

This is EQUIVALENT to minimizing the quadratic function:

$$f(x) = \frac{1}{2}x^T A x - b^T x$$
$A$
an $n \times n$ symmetric positive definite matrix (recall positive definiteness from Page 03 — all eigenvalues positive, the matrix defines a bowl shape)
$x$
the unknown vector we want to find (the solution)
$b$
a known right-hand-side vector
$f(x)$
a quadratic function (recall from Page 05) whose unique minimum is at $x^* = A^{-1}b$

Why are these equivalent? Take the gradient of $f$: $\nabla f(x) = Ax - b$. Setting the gradient to zero gives $Ax = b$. So finding where the quadratic bowl bottoms out is the same as solving the linear system.

Why CG is remarkable

For an $n$-dimensional problem, CG finds the exact solution in at most $n$ steps. Gradient descent might take thousands of steps (especially when the condition number of $A$ is large — recall from Page 01). Newton's method (Page 07) solves it in 1 step, but requires computing and inverting the Hessian — expensive for large problems. CG is the sweet spot: much faster than GD, much cheaper per step than Newton.

Part B: A-Conjugacy — The Key Concept

Physical analogy — Two workers painting different walls of a room.

Imagine two workers in a room. Worker 1 paints the north wall. Worker 2 paints the east wall. If they are working on truly independent walls, then Worker 2's painting does NOT mess up Worker 1's wall. They can work completely independently, and when both are done, both walls are perfectly painted.

Now imagine the room has a weird shape — the walls are slightly tilted, so painting the east wall causes some paint to splash onto the north wall. In this warped room, the workers are NOT independent. Worker 2's work partially undoes Worker 1's work.

A-conjugacy is the condition that makes two directions truly independent IN THE GEOMETRY DEFINED BY THE MATRIX $A$.

Ordinary orthogonality ($p_i^T p_j = 0$) means two directions are perpendicular in normal Euclidean space. But when we are minimizing $f(x) = \frac{1}{2}x^T A x - b^T x$, the matrix $A$ warps the geometry. What looks perpendicular in normal space might not be "independent" in the warped space. A-conjugacy ($p_i^T A p_j = 0$) means the directions are perpendicular in the warped geometry defined by $A$.

When two search directions are A-conjugate, minimizing along one does NOT change the optimal position along the other. This is why CG does not zigzag: each new direction is A-conjugate to all previous directions, so the progress made along previous directions is permanently locked in.
Definition: A-Conjugate (or $A$-Orthogonal) Directions $$p_i^T A\, p_j = 0 \quad \text{for all } i \neq j$$
$p_i, p_j$
two search direction vectors (different iterations of CG)
$A$
the SPD matrix from the system $Ax = b$
$A\,p_j$
the matrix $A$ applied to $p_j$ — this "warps" $p_j$ according to the geometry of the problem
$p_i^T A\,p_j$
the inner product of $p_i$ with the warped $p_j$ — if this is zero, the directions are A-conjugate
Special case: When $A = I$ (the identity matrix), $p_i^T A\,p_j = p_i^T I\, p_j = p_i^T p_j$. So A-conjugacy reduces to ordinary orthogonality. The identity matrix does not warp anything — the natural geometry and the $A$-geometry are the same.

Why conjugate directions solve the problem in $n$ steps

If you have $n$ mutually A-conjugate directions $p_0, p_1, \ldots, p_{n-1}$, they form a basis for $\mathbb{R}^n$ (they span all of $n$-dimensional space). The solution $x^*$ can be written as a combination of these directions. At each step, CG finds the exact coefficient for one direction by minimizing along it. Since the directions are A-conjugate, finding the coefficient for $p_k$ does not affect the already-found coefficients for $p_0, \ldots, p_{k-1}$. After $n$ steps, all coefficients are determined, and you have the exact solution.

Part C: The Linear CG Algorithm

Algorithm 5.2: Linear Conjugate Gradient (CG)
Given: SPD matrix $A$, right-hand side $b$, initial guess $x_0$, tolerance $\epsilon$

Initialize:
$r_0 = A x_0 - b$
// $r_0$ = the residual. "How far off is our current guess from satisfying $Ax = b$?" If $r_0 = 0$, we already have the answer.
$p_0 = -r_0$
// First search direction = negative residual = steepest descent direction (same as GD's first step)

for $k = 0, 1, 2, \ldots$

Step 1: $\alpha_k = -\dfrac{r_k^T p_k}{p_k^T A p_k}$
// Optimal step size: how far to walk along $p_k$ to minimize $f$. The numerator measures "how much downhill is left along $p_k$" and the denominator measures "how curved the function is along $p_k$."

Step 2: $x_{k+1} = x_k + \alpha_k\, p_k$
// Update the solution: move from current point $x_k$ by step $\alpha_k$ in direction $p_k$

Step 3: $r_{k+1} = r_k + \alpha_k\, A p_k$
// Update the residual. Equivalently: $r_{k+1} = A x_{k+1} - b$. The recurrence avoids re-multiplying $A x_{k+1}$.

Step 4: Check: if $\|r_{k+1}\| < \epsilon$, stop (converged!)
// If the residual is tiny, $Ax_{k+1} \approx b$ and we have found the solution.

Step 5: $\beta_{k+1} = \dfrac{r_{k+1}^T A p_k}{p_k^T A p_k}$
// The conjugacy coefficient. This determines how much of the OLD direction $p_k$ to mix into the NEW direction $p_{k+1}$ so that the new direction is A-conjugate to $p_k$.

Step 6: $p_{k+1} = -r_{k+1} + \beta_{k+1}\, p_k$
// New search direction = negative new residual (steepest descent part) + correction from old direction (conjugacy part). The $\beta$ term rotates the steepest descent direction just enough to make it A-conjugate to $p_k$.

end for

Why each formula works

$\alpha_k$ (step size): We want to minimize $f(x_k + \alpha p_k)$ over $\alpha$. For our quadratic $f$, this is a 1D quadratic in $\alpha$, and its minimum is at $\alpha_k = -\frac{r_k^T p_k}{p_k^T A p_k}$. This is an exact line search — we jump directly to the minimum along $p_k$.

$\beta_{k+1}$ (conjugacy coefficient): We want $p_{k+1}$ to be A-conjugate to $p_k$, meaning $p_{k+1}^T A p_k = 0$. Starting with the steepest descent direction $-r_{k+1}$ and adding a multiple $\beta_{k+1}$ of the old direction $p_k$, we choose $\beta_{k+1}$ so that the A-conjugacy condition holds. Solving for $\beta_{k+1}$ gives the formula above.

The genius of CG: Even though we want $p_{k+1}$ to be A-conjugate to ALL previous directions $p_0, \ldots, p_k$, it turns out that making it conjugate to just the last direction $p_k$ is enough. The math works out (thanks to the expanding subspace property) so that conjugacy with all previous directions comes for free. This is why CG only needs to store the current residual and the last direction — not the entire history.

Alternative formula for $\alpha_k$: Since $r_k = Ax_k - b = -\nabla f(x_k)$ and $p_0 = -r_0$, we can also write $\alpha_k = \frac{r_k^T r_k}{p_k^T A p_k}$ (using the identity $r_k^T p_k = -r_k^T r_k$ which holds for the CG algorithm). Both formulas give the same answer. The textbook uses $-\frac{r_k^T p_k}{p_k^T A p_k}$, so we use that here.

$$r_0 = \begin{bmatrix} 5 & 2 \\ 2 & 2 \end{bmatrix}\begin{bmatrix} 0 \\ 0 \end{bmatrix} - \begin{bmatrix} 7 \\ 4 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 0 \\ 0 \end{bmatrix} - \begin{bmatrix} 7 \\ 4 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} -7 \\ -4 \end{bmatrix}$$

The first CG direction is the same as steepest descent: point in the direction of $-r_0 = -\nabla f(x_0)$.

Compute $Ap_0$ (we will need this multiple times):

Part E: Why CG Works — The Geometry

Why GD zigzags but CG doesn't

Gradient descent: Each step points in the direction of steepest descent. For a quadratic, the gradient at $x_{k+1}$ is always perpendicular to the previous search direction $p_k$. This is a mathematical fact: the exact line search ensures $\nabla f(x_{k+1})^T p_k = 0$. So each new gradient is perpendicular to the last step — meaning you keep alternating between two nearly-opposite directions. On elongated contours (badly conditioned problems), this produces violent zigzagging.

Conjugate gradient: Each step direction is A-conjugate to all previous directions. Moving along $p_1$ does not undo progress along $p_0$. The "memory" of previous directions is encoded in the $\beta$ coefficient, which adjusts the steepest descent direction just enough to make it conjugate. No zigzag, no wasted work.

Part F: Properties and Comparison

Key Properties of the Linear CG Algorithm

1. Finite termination: For an $n \times n$ SPD system, CG terminates in at most $n$ iterations with the exact solution (in exact arithmetic).

2. Cost per iteration: One matrix-vector product $Ap_k$, plus a few dot products and vector additions. Total cost: $O(n^2)$ for a dense matrix, $O(\text{nnz})$ for a sparse matrix (where nnz = number of nonzeros in $A$). Compare to Gaussian elimination which costs $O(n^3)$.

3. Storage: Only need to store $x_k$, $r_k$, $p_k$, and $Ap_k$ — four vectors of length $n$. No need to store the matrix $A$ itself (only need to compute $Av$ for any vector $v$). This makes CG ideal for enormous sparse systems.

4. Convergence rate: The error after $k$ iterations satisfies $\|x_k - x^*\|_A \leq 2\left(\frac{\sqrt{\kappa} - 1}{\sqrt{\kappa} + 1}\right)^k \|x_0 - x^*\|_A$ where $\kappa = \lambda_{\max}/\lambda_{\min}$ is the condition number of $A$ (recall from Page 01). Better-conditioned matrices converge faster.

5. Monotonic decrease: $f(x_{k+1}) \leq f(x_k)$ at every step. CG never makes things worse.

The sweet spot of CG

CG occupies a beautiful middle ground. It is much smarter than gradient descent (finishes in $n$ steps instead of $\kappa$-dependent slow convergence), but much cheaper per step than Newton (no $O(n^3)$ system solve, no Hessian storage). For large sparse systems — think $n = 10{,}000$ or $n = 1{,}000{,}000$ — CG is often the method of choice because it only needs matrix-vector products, which are cheap for sparse matrices.

Looking ahead (Page 11): The CG algorithm above works for linear systems ($Ax = b$) and quadratic functions. But most real problems are nonlinear. Nonlinear CG extends the conjugate gradient idea to general smooth functions, using the same $\beta$ coefficient idea but with line search instead of the exact $\alpha$ formula.

Glossary

Residual ($r_k$)
$r_k = Ax_k - b$. Measures "how far off" the current guess is from satisfying the system. When $r_k = 0$, we have the exact solution. For the quadratic $f$, $r_k = \nabla f(x_k)$.
Search direction ($p_k$)
The direction CG moves along at iteration $k$. Chosen to be A-conjugate to all previous search directions.
A-conjugate (A-orthogonal)
Two vectors $p_i$ and $p_j$ are A-conjugate if $p_i^T A p_j = 0$. This means they are "independent" in the geometry warped by $A$, so minimizing along one does not undo progress along the other.
Step size ($\alpha_k$)
The optimal distance to move along $p_k$. Computed by exact line search on the quadratic: $\alpha_k = -r_k^T p_k / (p_k^T A p_k)$.
Conjugacy coefficient ($\beta_{k+1}$)
The amount of old direction $p_k$ to mix into the new direction $p_{k+1}$ so that A-conjugacy is maintained. Formula: $\beta_{k+1} = r_{k+1}^T A p_k / (p_k^T A p_k)$.
Symmetric Positive Definite (SPD)
A matrix $A$ that is symmetric ($A = A^T$) and positive definite (all eigenvalues positive, equivalently $x^T A x > 0$ for all $x \neq 0$). CG requires $A$ to be SPD. Recall Page 03.
Condition number ($\kappa$)
$\kappa(A) = \lambda_{\max} / \lambda_{\min}$. Controls how elongated the contours of $f$ are. Large $\kappa$ = elongated contours = GD zigzags badly, but CG still converges in at most $n$ steps.
Finite termination
CG solves an $n$-dimensional SPD system in at most $n$ iterations (in exact arithmetic). This is a theorem, not an approximation.
Matrix-vector product ($Ap_k$)
The most expensive operation per CG iteration. For sparse matrices, this costs $O(\text{nnz})$ where nnz is the number of nonzero entries. CG never needs to store or invert $A$ explicitly.
