Optimality Conditions

How to tell if you are at a minimum, maximum, or saddle; the theoretical backbone of every optimization algorithm

Part A: First-Order Necessary Conditions (FONC)

Physical analogy; The bottom of a valley is flat.

Imagine you are standing at the very bottom of a valley. You look north; the ground goes uphill. You look south; uphill. East; uphill. West; uphill. Every single direction goes uphill. That means, right where you stand, the ground is perfectly flat. There is zero slope in any direction.

Mathematically, "zero slope in every direction" means the gradient is the zero vector: $\nabla f(x^*) = \mathbf{0}$.

But here is the catch: flat ground does not GUARANTEE you are at a valley bottom! You could also be standing on:
• The very top of a hill (also flat at the peak)
• A perfectly level mountain pass (a saddle; flat at the center but curving up in some directions and down in others)

So $\nabla f = \mathbf{0}$ is necessary for a minimum (it MUST be true), but NOT sufficient (it alone does not PROVE you are at a minimum). We need to check curvature too, that is, Part B.
Theorem 1: First-Order Necessary Conditions (FONC)

If $x^*$ is a local minimizer of $f$ and $\nabla f$ is continuous in a neighborhood of $x^*$, then:

$$\nabla f(x^*) = \mathbf{0}$$
$x^*$
the candidate point; the location we are testing
$\nabla f(x^*)$
the gradient evaluated at $x^*$ (recall from Page 02: the vector of all partial derivatives)
$\mathbf{0}$
the zero vector; all components are zero, meaning no slope in any direction
local minimizer
a point where $f(x^*) \leq f(x)$ for all $x$ sufficiently close to $x^*$

In plain English: At a local minimum, the gradient must be zero. If the gradient is NOT zero at some point, that point is definitely NOT a local minimum.

Why This Matters for Algorithms

This is the reason EVERY optimization algorithm looks for points where $\nabla f = \mathbf{0}$. The standard stopping criterion is:

$$\|\nabla f(x_k)\| < \epsilon$$

where $\epsilon$ is a small tolerance (like $10^{-6}$). This checks: "is the gradient close enough to zero that we are probably near a stationary point?" Every algorithm on pages 06 through 14 uses this stopping rule.

Terminology: "Stationary point" vs "minimizer"
A stationary point is any point where $\nabla f = \mathbf{0}$. It could be a minimum, maximum, or saddle.
A minimizer is a stationary point that is actually a minimum.
Every minimizer is a stationary point. Not every stationary point is a minimizer.

Part B: Second-Order Conditions (SONC and SOSC)

Physical analogy; Flat ground plus curvature tells the whole story.

You know the ground is flat where you stand (gradient = zero). Now you need to feel the curvature. Kneel down and feel the ground in every direction:

Curving upward in EVERY direction? You are in a valley. This is a minimum. The Hessian (the curvature matrix from Page 02) is positive definite.

Curving downward in EVERY direction? You are on a hilltop. This is a maximum. The Hessian is negative definite.

Curving up in some directions, down in others? You are on a mountain pass (saddle). The Hessian is indefinite; it has both positive and negative eigenvalues (see Page 03).

Flat in some direction? Inconclusive; the second-order test cannot decide. You would need higher-order derivatives.

Theorem 2: Second-Order Necessary Conditions (SONC)

If $x^*$ is a local minimizer and $\nabla^2 f$ is continuous near $x^*$, then:

$$\nabla f(x^*) = \mathbf{0} \quad \text{AND} \quad \nabla^2 f(x^*) \text{ is positive semidefinite (PSD)}$$
$\nabla^2 f(x^*)$
the Hessian matrix evaluated at $x^*$ (the matrix of all second partial derivatives)
PSD
all eigenvalues $\geq 0$ (curvature is non-negative in every direction)

"Necessary" means: If $x^*$ IS a minimum, then these conditions MUST hold. But satisfying them does NOT guarantee $x^*$ IS a minimum. (The ground could be flat in some direction, that is, PSD but not PD.)

Theorem 3: Second-Order Sufficient Conditions (SOSC)

If $\nabla^2 f$ is continuous near $x^*$ and:

$$\nabla f(x^*) = \mathbf{0} \quad \text{AND} \quad \nabla^2 f(x^*) \text{ is positive definite (PD)}$$

then $x^*$ is a strict local minimizer.

PD
all eigenvalues strictly $> 0$ (curvature is strictly positive in EVERY direction)
strict local minimizer
$f(x^*) < f(x)$ for all $x \neq x^*$ nearby (it is strictly the lowest point in a neighborhood)

"Sufficient" means: If these conditions are satisfied, then $x^*$ IS guaranteed to be a local minimum. This is the PROOF that you are at a minimum.

Necessary vs Sufficient; a critical distinction:

Necessary condition: "If you are a minimum, then THIS must be true." (Direction: minimum $\Rightarrow$ condition)
Sufficient condition: "If THIS is true, then you are guaranteed to be a minimum." (Direction: condition $\Rightarrow$ minimum)

Example from everyday life:
• Necessary for driving: you must have a car. (If you are driving, you have a car.)
• Sufficient for driving: having a car, a license, fuel, and keys. (If you have all of these, you CAN drive.)
• Having a car alone is necessary but NOT sufficient (you also need fuel, keys, etc.)

Same idea: gradient = 0 is necessary but not sufficient. Gradient = 0 AND Hessian PD is sufficient.

Theorem 4: Second-Order Sufficient Conditions for Maximizer

If $\nabla^2 f$ is continuous near $x^*$ and:

$$\nabla f(x^*) = \mathbf{0} \quad \text{AND} \quad \nabla^2 f(x^*) \text{ is negative definite (ND)}$$

then $x^*$ is a strict local maximizer.

Same logic: all eigenvalues are strictly negative means the surface curves downward in every direction from $x^*$, making it a hilltop.

Summary: The Decision Rules
GradientHessianConclusion
$\nabla f \neq 0$N/ANot a stationary point. Move on.
$\nabla f = 0$PD (all $\lambda_i > 0$)Strict local minimum (SOSC)
$\nabla f = 0$ND (all $\lambda_i < 0$)Strict local maximum
$\nabla f = 0$Indefinite (mixed signs)Saddle point
$\nabla f = 0$PSD (some $\lambda_i = 0$, rest $> 0$)Inconclusive; need more info

Part C: Full Classification Example; Mirrors Quiz 2

Classify All Stationary Points of $f(x_1, x_2) = 2x_1^3 - 3x_1^2 - 6x_1 x_2(x_1 - x_2 - 1)$

This mirrors the Quiz 2 function. We will go through every step: find stationary points, compute the Hessian at each one, check eigenvalues, and classify.

Step 1: Expand the function
First, expand $-6x_1 x_2(x_1 - x_2 - 1)$: $$-6x_1 x_2 \cdot x_1 + 6x_1 x_2 \cdot x_2 + 6x_1 x_2 \cdot 1 = -6x_1^2 x_2 + 6x_1 x_2^2 + 6x_1 x_2$$ So the full function is: $$f(x_1, x_2) = 2x_1^3 - 3x_1^2 - 6x_1^2 x_2 + 6x_1 x_2^2 + 6x_1 x_2$$
Step 2: Compute the gradient (set $\nabla f = 0$)
$$\frac{\partial f}{\partial x_1} = 6x_1^2 - 6x_1 - 12x_1 x_2 + 6x_2^2 + 6x_2 = 0$$ $$\frac{\partial f}{\partial x_2} = -6x_1^2 + 12x_1 x_2 + 6x_1 = 0$$ From the second equation, factor out $6x_1$: $$6x_1(-x_1 + 2x_2 + 1) = 0$$ So either $x_1 = 0$ or $x_1 = 2x_2 + 1$.
Step 3: Solve for stationary points
Case 1: $x_1 = 0$. Substitute into the first equation: $$0 - 0 - 0 + 6x_2^2 + 6x_2 = 0 \implies 6x_2(x_2 + 1) = 0$$ So $x_2 = 0$ or $x_2 = -1$. Stationary points: $(0, 0)$ and $(0, -1)$.

Case 2: $x_1 = 2x_2 + 1$. Substitute into the first equation: $$6(2x_2+1)^2 - 6(2x_2+1) - 12(2x_2+1)x_2 + 6x_2^2 + 6x_2 = 0$$ Expand $(2x_2+1)^2 = 4x_2^2 + 4x_2 + 1$: $$24x_2^2 + 24x_2 + 6 - 12x_2 - 6 - 24x_2^2 - 12x_2 + 6x_2^2 + 6x_2 = 0$$ $$6x_2^2 + 6x_2 = 0 \implies 6x_2(x_2 + 1) = 0$$ So $x_2 = 0 \Rightarrow x_1 = 1$, or $x_2 = -1 \Rightarrow x_1 = -1$.
Stationary points: $(1, 0)$ and $(-1, -1)$.
Step 4: Compute the Hessian
$$\nabla^2 f = \begin{bmatrix} \frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial x_1^2} & \frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial x_1 \partial x_2} \\ \frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial x_2 \partial x_1} & \frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial x_2^2} \end{bmatrix}$$ $$\frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial x_1^2} = 12x_1 - 6 - 12x_2$$ $$\frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial x_1 \partial x_2} = -12x_1 + 12x_2 + 6$$ $$\frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial x_2^2} = 12x_1$$ Wait; let us recompute $\frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial x_2^2}$ more carefully. From $\frac{\partial f}{\partial x_2} = -6x_1^2 + 12x_1 x_2 + 6x_1$: $$\frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial x_2^2} = 12x_1$$ So: $$\nabla^2 f = \begin{bmatrix} 12x_1 - 6 - 12x_2 & -12x_1 + 12x_2 + 6 \\ -12x_1 + 12x_2 + 6 & 12x_1 \end{bmatrix}$$
Step 5: Evaluate at each stationary point and classify
Point $(0, 0)$: $$H = \begin{bmatrix} -6 & 6 \\ 6 & 0 \end{bmatrix}$$ $\det = (-6)(0) - (6)(6) = -36 < 0$ $\Rightarrow$ Indefinite = Saddle Point

Point $(0, -1)$: $$H = \begin{bmatrix} -6 + 12 & 0 - 12 + 6 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 6 & -6 \\ -6 & 0 \end{bmatrix}$$ $\det = (6)(0) - (-6)(-6) = -36 < 0$ $\Rightarrow$ Indefinite = Saddle Point

Point $(1, 0)$: $$H = \begin{bmatrix} 12 - 6 & -12 + 6 \\ -12 + 6 & 12 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 6 & -6 \\ -6 & 12 \end{bmatrix}$$ $\det = (6)(12) - (-6)(-6) = 72 - 36 = 36 > 0$ and $a_{11} = 6 > 0$ $\Rightarrow$ PD = Local Minimum

Point $(-1, -1)$: $$H = \begin{bmatrix} -12 - 6 + 12 & 12 - 12 + 6 \\ 12 - 12 + 6 & -12 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} -6 & 6 \\ 6 & -12 \end{bmatrix}$$ $\det = (-6)(-12) - (6)(6) = 72 - 36 = 36 > 0$ and $a_{11} = -6 < 0$ $\Rightarrow$ ND = Local Maximum
Stationary PointHessiandet(H)$a_{11}$EigenvaluesClassification
$(0, 0)$ $\begin{bmatrix} -6 & 6 \\ 6 & 0 \end{bmatrix}$ $-36$ $-6$ $+6\sqrt{2}, -6\sqrt{2}$ Saddle
$(0, -1)$ $\begin{bmatrix} 6 & -6 \\ -6 & 0 \end{bmatrix}$ $-36$ $6$ $3+3\sqrt{5}, 3-3\sqrt{5}$ Saddle
$(1, 0)$ $\begin{bmatrix} 6 & -6 \\ -6 & 12 \end{bmatrix}$ $36$ $6$ $9+3\sqrt{5}, 9-3\sqrt{5}$ Local Min
$(-1, -1)$ $\begin{bmatrix} -6 & 6 \\ 6 & -12 \end{bmatrix}$ $36$ $-6$ $-9+3\sqrt{5}, -9-3\sqrt{5}$ Local Max
How the eigenvalues were found (for the curious):
For $(1, 0)$ with $H = \begin{bmatrix} 6 & -6 \\ -6 & 12 \end{bmatrix}$: trace $= 18$, det $= 36$.
$\lambda^2 - 18\lambda + 36 = 0$.
$\lambda = \frac{18 \pm \sqrt{324 - 144}}{2} = \frac{18 \pm \sqrt{180}}{2} = \frac{18 \pm 6\sqrt{5}}{2} = 9 \pm 3\sqrt{5}$.
Since $\sqrt{5} \approx 2.236$: $\lambda_1 \approx 15.71$ and $\lambda_2 \approx 2.29$. Both positive, so PD.

Part D: The Classification Flowchart

The subtle case: Hessian = zero matrix.

Consider $f(x_1, x_2) = (x_2 - x_1^2)^2 + x_1^5$ from Quiz 2, Question 2. At $(0, 0)$:
  • $\nabla f(0,0) = \mathbf{0}$ ✓ (it is stationary)
  • $\nabla^2 f(0,0) = \begin{bmatrix} 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 2 \end{bmatrix}$; eigenvalues are 0 and 2
  • This is positive semidefinite (eigenvalues $\geq 0$), so the second-order test is inconclusive
It turns out this point IS a saddle (because of the $x_1^5$ term), but you cannot determine that from the Hessian alone. You would need to look at higher-order behavior. The takeaway: when the Hessian is singular (has zero eigenvalues), more work is needed.

Part E: Connecting Optimality to Algorithms

How Each Algorithm Uses These Conditions

Every algorithm in this course is ultimately trying to find points that satisfy the optimality conditions above. Here is how each one connects:

Algorithm-Condition Connection Map

The pattern: Simple algorithms (gradient descent) only use first-order conditions. Sophisticated algorithms (Newton, BFGS, trust region) also use second-order information, which is why they converge faster; they check not just "is the ground flat?" but also "does the ground curve upward?"

Quick Practice: Classify Without Full Computation

For each Hessian, determine the classification using the 2×2 shortcut from Page 03.

(a) $H = \begin{bmatrix} 4 & 1 \\ 1 & 3 \end{bmatrix}$
$a_{11} = 4 > 0$, $\det = 12 - 1 = 11 > 0$ $\Rightarrow$ PD = local minimum
(b) $H = \begin{bmatrix} -2 & 1 \\ 1 & -5 \end{bmatrix}$
$a_{11} = -2 < 0$, $\det = 10 - 1 = 9 > 0$ $\Rightarrow$ ND = local maximum
(c) $H = \begin{bmatrix} 3 & 4 \\ 4 & 2 \end{bmatrix}$
$\det = 6 - 16 = -10 < 0$ $\Rightarrow$ Indefinite = saddle point
(d) $H = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 1 \\ 1 & 1 \end{bmatrix}$
$\det = 1 - 1 = 0$, eigenvalues are 0 and 2. PSD but not PD $\Rightarrow$ Inconclusive
Practice tip: on an exam, the 2×2 shortcut ($a_{11}$ sign and $\det$ sign) is faster than computing eigenvalues. Use eigenvalues only when the question asks for them explicitly, or when the shortcut is inconclusive.
Common exam mistake: Checking only the diagonal entries of the Hessian. The matrix $\begin{bmatrix} 3 & 4 \\ 4 & 2 \end{bmatrix}$ has positive diagonals (3 and 2) but is INDEFINITE because the off-diagonal entries are large enough to make $\det < 0$. Always check the determinant, not just the diagonal!

The Big Picture: What We Can and Cannot Determine

SituationWhat We KnowWhat We Cannot Know
$\nabla f \neq 0$Definitely NOT a min/max/saddleN/A; move on
$\nabla f = 0$, $H$ PDGuaranteed local min (SOSC)Whether it is the global min (need convexity, Page 05)
$\nabla f = 0$, $H$ NDGuaranteed local maxWhether it is the global max
$\nabla f = 0$, $H$ indefiniteGuaranteed saddleN/A; classification complete
$\nabla f = 0$, $H$ singularStationary, but type unknownNeed higher-order analysis

The second-order test cannot distinguish between a flat-bottomed minimum and a saddle when the Hessian has zero eigenvalues. This is rare on exams but important to know → see the $f = (x_2 - x_1^2)^2 + x_1^5$ example above.

Glossary

Stationary Point (Critical Point)
A point $x^*$ where $\nabla f(x^*) = \mathbf{0}$. Could be a min, max, or saddle.
Local Minimizer
A point $x^*$ where $f(x^*) \leq f(x)$ for all $x$ in some neighborhood of $x^*$. "The lowest point nearby."
Global Minimizer
A point $x^*$ where $f(x^*) \leq f(x)$ for ALL $x$ in the domain. "The absolute lowest point anywhere." For convex functions (see Page 05), local = global.
Local Maximizer
A point $x^*$ where $f(x^*) \geq f(x)$ for all $x$ nearby. The highest point in the neighborhood.
Saddle Point
A stationary point that is neither a local min nor a local max. The function increases in some directions and decreases in others.
Necessary Condition
A condition that MUST be true if you are at a minimum. Failing it proves you are NOT at a minimum. Passing it does not guarantee you ARE at a minimum.
Sufficient Condition
A condition that GUARANTEES you are at a minimum. Passing it proves you ARE at a minimum. Failing it does not prove you are NOT at a minimum (you might still be at a degenerate minimum).
FONC (First-Order Necessary Conditions)
$\nabla f(x^*) = \mathbf{0}$. The gradient is zero at any minimizer. (Theorem 1)
SONC (Second-Order Necessary Conditions)
$\nabla f(x^*) = \mathbf{0}$ and $\nabla^2 f(x^*)$ is PSD. (Theorem 2)
SOSC (Second-Order Sufficient Conditions)
$\nabla f(x^*) = \mathbf{0}$ and $\nabla^2 f(x^*)$ is PD $\Rightarrow$ strict local minimum. (Theorem 3)
Classification
The process of determining whether a stationary point is a min, max, or saddle, using the eigenvalues of the Hessian.
