Part A: Gradient Descent with Exact Line Search

Core method from Page 06. Mirrors Assignment 1 style.

Problem: Consider $f(x_1, x_2) = 3x_1^2 + 2x_2^2$. Starting from $x_0 = \begin{bmatrix} 2 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix}$, perform one iteration of gradient descent with exact line search.

Full Worked Solution

Step 1: Compute the gradient at $x_0$
The gradient is $\nabla f(x) = \begin{bmatrix} 6x_1 \\ 4x_2 \end{bmatrix}$ (recall: differentiate each variable, Page 02). $$\nabla f(x_0) = \nabla f\!\left(\begin{bmatrix} 2 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix}\right) = \begin{bmatrix} 6(2) \\ 4(1) \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 12 \\ 4 \end{bmatrix}$$
Step 2: Set the search direction
The steepest descent direction is $d_0 = -\nabla f(x_0)$: $$d_0 = -\begin{bmatrix} 12 \\ 4 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} -12 \\ -4 \end{bmatrix}$$
Step 3: Compute the exact step size
We need to minimize $\phi(\alpha) = f(x_0 + \alpha d_0)$ over $\alpha \gt 0$. The point along the line is: $$x_0 + \alpha d_0 = \begin{bmatrix} 2 - 12\alpha \\ 1 - 4\alpha \end{bmatrix}$$ Substituting into $f$: $$\phi(\alpha) = 3(2 - 12\alpha)^2 + 2(1 - 4\alpha)^2$$ $$= 3(4 - 48\alpha + 144\alpha^2) + 2(1 - 8\alpha + 16\alpha^2)$$ $$= 12 - 144\alpha + 432\alpha^2 + 2 - 16\alpha + 32\alpha^2$$ $$= 14 - 160\alpha + 464\alpha^2$$ Set $\phi'(\alpha) = 0$: $$\phi'(\alpha) = -160 + 928\alpha = 0$$ $$\alpha_0 = \frac{160}{928} = \frac{5}{29}$$

Shortcut check: For quadratics $f(x) = \frac{1}{2}x^T A x$ (with $A = \begin{bmatrix}6 & 0 \\ 0 & 4\end{bmatrix}$), the exact step size formula is $\alpha = \frac{g^T g}{g^T A g}$. We have $g^T g = 144 + 16 = 160$ and $g^T A g = \begin{bmatrix}12 & 4\end{bmatrix}\begin{bmatrix}72\\16\end{bmatrix} = 864 + 64 = 928$. So $\alpha = 160/928 = 5/29$. Matches!

Step 4: Update the point
$$x_1 = x_0 + \alpha_0 d_0 = \begin{bmatrix} 2 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix} + \frac{5}{29}\begin{bmatrix} -12 \\ -4 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 2 - 60/29 \\ 1 - 20/29 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} -2/29 \\ 9/29 \end{bmatrix}$$
$$x_1 = \begin{bmatrix} -2/29 \\ 9/29 \end{bmatrix} \approx \begin{bmatrix} -0.069 \\ 0.310 \end{bmatrix}$$
Step 5: Verify; function value decreased
$$f(x_0) = 3(4) + 2(1) = 14$$ $$f(x_1) = 3\left(\frac{4}{841}\right) + 2\left(\frac{81}{841}\right) = \frac{12 + 162}{841} = \frac{174}{841} \approx 0.207$$

Function value dropped from 14 to 0.207. Gradient descent made huge progress in one step.

Part B: Newton's Method for Optimization

Core method from Page 07. Mirrors Assignment 1 style.

Problem: Consider $f(x_1, x_2) = x_1^4 + x_2^2 - 2x_1^2$. Starting from $x_0 = \begin{bmatrix} 2 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix}$, perform one Newton step.

Full Worked Solution

Step 1: Compute the gradient at $x_0$
$$\nabla f(x) = \begin{bmatrix} 4x_1^3 - 4x_1 \\ 2x_2 \end{bmatrix}$$ $$\nabla f(x_0) = \begin{bmatrix} 4(8) - 4(2) \\ 2(1) \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 32 - 8 \\ 2 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 24 \\ 2 \end{bmatrix}$$
Step 2: Compute the Hessian at $x_0$
The Hessian is the matrix of second partial derivatives (Page 02): $$\nabla^2 f(x) = \begin{bmatrix} 12x_1^2 - 4 & 0 \\ 0 & 2 \end{bmatrix}$$ $$\nabla^2 f(x_0) = \begin{bmatrix} 12(4) - 4 & 0 \\ 0 & 2 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 44 & 0 \\ 0 & 2 \end{bmatrix}$$

Check: is the Hessian positive definite? Eigenvalues are 44 and 2 (diagonal matrix). Both positive, so yes; Newton step is a descent direction.

Step 3: Solve the Newton system $\nabla^2 f(x_0)\, p_0 = -\nabla f(x_0)$
$$\begin{bmatrix} 44 & 0 \\ 0 & 2 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} p_1 \\ p_2 \end{bmatrix} = -\begin{bmatrix} 24 \\ 2 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} -24 \\ -2 \end{bmatrix}$$ Since the Hessian is diagonal, this is trivial: $$p_1 = -24/44 = -6/11, \qquad p_2 = -2/2 = -1$$ $$p_0 = \begin{bmatrix} -6/11 \\ -1 \end{bmatrix}$$
Step 4: Update the point (with step size $\alpha = 1$)
Newton's method uses $\alpha = 1$ (the full step): $$x_1 = x_0 + p_0 = \begin{bmatrix} 2 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix} + \begin{bmatrix} -6/11 \\ -1 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 16/11 \\ 0 \end{bmatrix}$$
$$x_1 = \begin{bmatrix} 16/11 \\ 0 \end{bmatrix} \approx \begin{bmatrix} 1.455 \\ 0 \end{bmatrix}$$
Step 5: Verify
$$f(x_0) = 16 + 1 - 8 = 9$$ $$f(x_1) = (16/11)^4 + 0 - 2(16/11)^2 = \frac{65536}{14641} - \frac{512}{121} = \frac{65536 - 61952}{14641} = \frac{3584}{14641} \approx 0.245$$

Function value dropped from 9 to about 0.245. Note that $f$ has minimizers at $(\pm 1, 0)$ with $f = -1$, so Newton is heading in the right direction but hasn't arrived yet since $f$ is not quadratic. A second Newton step would get much closer.

Part C: Trust-Region Method

Core method from Page 09. Mirrors Quiz 4 format exactly.

Problem: Consider $f(x_1, x_2) = x_1^2 + 4x_2^2 + x_1 x_2$. At $x_0 = \begin{bmatrix} 2 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix}$ with trust-region radius $\Delta_0 = 1$, parameters $\eta = 0.25$.

(a) Write the quadratic model $m_0(p)$.
(b) Compute the Newton step $p^N$. Is it inside the trust region?
(c) If the Newton step is outside, compute the Cauchy point.
(d) Suppose we take step $p = \begin{bmatrix} -0.7 \\ -0.3 \end{bmatrix}$. Compute $\rho_0$ and decide whether to accept the step and how to update $\Delta$.

Full Worked Solution

Step 1: Compute gradient and Hessian
$$\nabla f(x) = \begin{bmatrix} 2x_1 + x_2 \\ 8x_2 + x_1 \end{bmatrix}, \qquad \nabla^2 f(x) = \begin{bmatrix} 2 & 1 \\ 1 & 8 \end{bmatrix}$$ At $x_0 = (2, 1)$: $$g_0 = \nabla f(x_0) = \begin{bmatrix} 5 \\ 10 \end{bmatrix}, \qquad B_0 = \nabla^2 f(x_0) = \begin{bmatrix} 2 & 1 \\ 1 & 8 \end{bmatrix}$$ Note: the Hessian is constant here since $f$ is quadratic. Also $f(x_0) = 4 + 4 + 2 = 10$.
Step 2 (a): Write the quadratic model
$$m_0(p) = f(x_0) + g_0^T p + \frac{1}{2} p^T B_0 p$$ $$= 10 + \begin{bmatrix} 5 & 10 \end{bmatrix} p + \frac{1}{2} p^T \begin{bmatrix} 2 & 1 \\ 1 & 8 \end{bmatrix} p$$
Step 3 (b): Compute the Newton step
Solve $B_0 p^N = -g_0$: $$\begin{bmatrix} 2 & 1 \\ 1 & 8 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} p_1 \\ p_2 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} -5 \\ -10 \end{bmatrix}$$ Using the $2 \times 2$ inverse formula (Page 03): $\det(B) = 16 - 1 = 15$. $$B_0^{-1} = \frac{1}{15}\begin{bmatrix} 8 & -1 \\ -1 & 2 \end{bmatrix}$$ $$p^N = -B_0^{-1} g_0 = -\frac{1}{15}\begin{bmatrix} 8 & -1 \\ -1 & 2 \end{bmatrix}\begin{bmatrix} 5 \\ 10 \end{bmatrix} = -\frac{1}{15}\begin{bmatrix} 30 \\ 15 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} -2 \\ -1 \end{bmatrix}$$ Check: $\|p^N\| = \sqrt{4 + 1} = \sqrt{5} \approx 2.236$.
Since $\|p^N\| = \sqrt{5} \approx 2.236 \gt \Delta_0 = 1$, the Newton step is OUTSIDE the trust region. We cannot take it directly.
Step 4 (c): Compute the Cauchy point
The Cauchy point is the steepest descent step restricted to the trust region. First, compute the unconstrained steepest descent step size: $$\alpha^C = \frac{g_0^T g_0}{g_0^T B_0 g_0}$$ $$g_0^T g_0 = 25 + 100 = 125$$ $$B_0 g_0 = \begin{bmatrix} 2 & 1 \\ 1 & 8 \end{bmatrix}\begin{bmatrix} 5 \\ 10 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 20 \\ 85 \end{bmatrix}$$ $$g_0^T B_0 g_0 = 5(20) + 10(85) = 100 + 850 = 950$$ $$\alpha^C = \frac{125}{950} = \frac{5}{38}$$ The unconstrained Cauchy step is $p^C_{\text{uncon}} = -\alpha^C g_0 = -\frac{5}{38}\begin{bmatrix}5\\10\end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix}-25/38\\-50/38\end{bmatrix}$. $\|p^C_{\text{uncon}}\| = \frac{5}{38}\sqrt{125} = \frac{5 \cdot 5\sqrt{5}}{38} = \frac{25\sqrt{5}}{38} \approx 1.471$. Since $1.471 \gt \Delta_0 = 1$, the unconstrained Cauchy step is also outside the trust region. So we scale it to the boundary: $$p^C = -\frac{\Delta_0}{\|g_0\|} g_0 = -\frac{1}{\sqrt{125}}\begin{bmatrix}5\\10\end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} -5/\sqrt{125} \\ -10/\sqrt{125} \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} -\sqrt{5}/5 \\ -2\sqrt{5}/5 \end{bmatrix} \approx \begin{bmatrix} -0.447 \\ -0.894 \end{bmatrix}$$
Step 5 (d): Compute $\rho_0$ for the given step
Given $p = \begin{bmatrix}-0.7\\-0.3\end{bmatrix}$. First compute the actual reduction: $$f(x_0 + p) = f\!\left(\begin{bmatrix}1.3\\0.7\end{bmatrix}\right) = (1.3)^2 + 4(0.7)^2 + (1.3)(0.7) = 1.69 + 1.96 + 0.91 = 4.56$$ $$\text{actual reduction} = f(x_0) - f(x_0 + p) = 10 - 4.56 = 5.44$$ Now compute the predicted reduction from the model: $$m_0(p) = 10 + g_0^T p + \frac{1}{2}p^T B_0 p$$ $$g_0^T p = 5(-0.7) + 10(-0.3) = -3.5 - 3.0 = -6.5$$ $$B_0 p = \begin{bmatrix}2&1\\1&8\end{bmatrix}\begin{bmatrix}-0.7\\-0.3\end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix}-1.7\\-3.1\end{bmatrix}$$ $$p^T B_0 p = (-0.7)(-1.7) + (-0.3)(-3.1) = 1.19 + 0.93 = 2.12$$ $$m_0(p) = 10 - 6.5 + \frac{1}{2}(2.12) = 10 - 6.5 + 1.06 = 4.56$$ $$\text{predicted reduction} = m_0(0) - m_0(p) = 10 - 4.56 = 5.44$$ $$\rho_0 = \frac{\text{actual reduction}}{\text{predicted reduction}} = \frac{5.44}{5.44} = 1.0$$
$\rho_0 = 1.0$; the model perfectly predicted the actual reduction!

This makes sense: $f$ is quadratic, and the quadratic model of a quadratic function is exact. So the model always perfectly predicts the actual change.

Step 6 (d): Decision
Since $\rho_0 = 1.0 \gt \eta = 0.25$: Accept the step ($x_1 = x_0 + p = [1.3, 0.7]^T$). Since $\rho_0 \gt 0.75$ and $\|p\| = \sqrt{0.49 + 0.09} = \sqrt{0.58} \approx 0.762 \lt \Delta_0 = 1$, the step did not hit the boundary, so we keep $\Delta_1 = \Delta_0 = 1$ (no need to expand since we didn't use the full radius).

Common trust-region update rules: if $\rho \lt 0.25$, shrink $\Delta$ (poor model). If $\rho \gt 0.75$ AND step hit boundary, expand $\Delta$. Otherwise keep $\Delta$ the same.

Part D: Conjugate Gradient (Linear CG)

Core method from Page 10. Mirrors Quiz 5 format exactly.

Problem: Solve $Ax = b$ using CG, where $$A = \begin{bmatrix} 4 & 1 \\ 1 & 3 \end{bmatrix}, \qquad b = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 2 \end{bmatrix}, \qquad x_0 = \begin{bmatrix} 0 \\ 0 \end{bmatrix}$$ Run 2 iterations of the CG algorithm.

Full Worked Solution

Initialization
$$r_0 = b - Ax_0 = \begin{bmatrix}1\\2\end{bmatrix} - \begin{bmatrix}0\\0\end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix}1\\2\end{bmatrix}$$ $$d_0 = r_0 = \begin{bmatrix}1\\2\end{bmatrix}$$ (The first search direction is the residual, which equals the steepest descent direction.)
Iteration 1: Compute $\alpha_0$
$$\alpha_0 = \frac{r_0^T r_0}{d_0^T A d_0}$$ $$r_0^T r_0 = 1 + 4 = 5$$ $$Ad_0 = \begin{bmatrix}4&1\\1&3\end{bmatrix}\begin{bmatrix}1\\2\end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix}6\\7\end{bmatrix}$$ $$d_0^T A d_0 = 1(6) + 2(7) = 6 + 14 = 20$$ $$\alpha_0 = \frac{5}{20} = \frac{1}{4}$$
Iteration 1: Update $x$ and $r$
$$x_1 = x_0 + \alpha_0 d_0 = \begin{bmatrix}0\\0\end{bmatrix} + \frac{1}{4}\begin{bmatrix}1\\2\end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix}1/4\\1/2\end{bmatrix}$$ $$r_1 = r_0 - \alpha_0 A d_0 = \begin{bmatrix}1\\2\end{bmatrix} - \frac{1}{4}\begin{bmatrix}6\\7\end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix}1 - 3/2\\2 - 7/4\end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix}-1/2\\1/4\end{bmatrix}$$
Iteration 1: Compute $\beta_0$ and new direction
$$\beta_0 = \frac{r_1^T r_1}{r_0^T r_0} = \frac{1/4 + 1/16}{5} = \frac{5/16}{5} = \frac{1}{16}$$ $$d_1 = r_1 + \beta_0 d_0 = \begin{bmatrix}-1/2\\1/4\end{bmatrix} + \frac{1}{16}\begin{bmatrix}1\\2\end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix}-1/2 + 1/16\\1/4 + 2/16\end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix}-7/16\\3/8\end{bmatrix}$$

Orthogonality check: $r_1^T r_0 = (-1/2)(1) + (1/4)(2) = -1/2 + 1/2 = 0$. The residuals are orthogonal, as CG guarantees.

Iteration 2: Compute $\alpha_1$
$$r_1^T r_1 = 1/4 + 1/16 = 5/16$$ $$Ad_1 = \begin{bmatrix}4&1\\1&3\end{bmatrix}\begin{bmatrix}-7/16\\3/8\end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix}-28/16 + 3/8\\-7/16 + 9/8\end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix}-28/16 + 6/16\\-7/16 + 18/16\end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix}-22/16\\11/16\end{bmatrix}$$ $$= \begin{bmatrix}-11/8\\11/16\end{bmatrix}$$ $$d_1^T A d_1 = \left(-\frac{7}{16}\right)\left(-\frac{11}{8}\right) + \left(\frac{3}{8}\right)\left(\frac{11}{16}\right) = \frac{77}{128} + \frac{33}{128} = \frac{110}{128} = \frac{55}{64}$$ $$\alpha_1 = \frac{r_1^T r_1}{d_1^T A d_1} = \frac{5/16}{55/64} = \frac{5}{16} \cdot \frac{64}{55} = \frac{320}{880} = \frac{4}{11}$$
Iteration 2: Update $x$
$$x_2 = x_1 + \alpha_1 d_1 = \begin{bmatrix}1/4\\1/2\end{bmatrix} + \frac{4}{11}\begin{bmatrix}-7/16\\3/8\end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix}1/4 - 7/44\\1/2 + 3/22\end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix}11/44 - 7/44\\11/22 + 3/22\end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix}4/44\\14/22\end{bmatrix}$$ $$= \begin{bmatrix}1/11\\7/11\end{bmatrix}$$
$$x_2 = \begin{bmatrix}1/11\\7/11\end{bmatrix}$$
Verification: is $x_2$ the exact solution?
$$Ax_2 = \begin{bmatrix}4&1\\1&3\end{bmatrix}\begin{bmatrix}1/11\\7/11\end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix}4/11 + 7/11\\1/11 + 21/11\end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix}11/11\\22/11\end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix}1\\2\end{bmatrix} = b$$

CG solved the $2 \times 2$ system in exactly 2 iterations, as the theory guarantees ($n$ steps for an $n$-dimensional problem).

Part E: BFGS Method

Core method from Page 12. Mirrors Quiz 6 format exactly.

Problem: Consider $f(x_1, x_2) = 2x_1^2 + x_2^2 - 2x_1 x_2$, $x_0 = \begin{bmatrix}2\\1\end{bmatrix}$, $H_0 = I$ (initial inverse Hessian approximation). Perform one BFGS iteration with exact line search.

Full Worked Solution

Step 1: Compute gradient at $x_0$
$$\nabla f(x) = \begin{bmatrix}4x_1 - 2x_2\\2x_2 - 2x_1\end{bmatrix}$$ $$g_0 = \nabla f(x_0) = \begin{bmatrix}4(2)-2(1)\\2(1)-2(2)\end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix}6\\-2\end{bmatrix}$$
Step 2: Compute search direction
$$d_0 = -H_0 g_0 = -I \begin{bmatrix}6\\-2\end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix}-6\\2\end{bmatrix}$$
Step 3: Exact line search for $\alpha_0$
The Hessian of $f$ is $A = \begin{bmatrix}4&-2\\-2&2\end{bmatrix}$. Since $f$ is quadratic: $f(x) = \frac{1}{2}x^T A x$ (with no linear term since the minimum is at origin). $$\alpha_0 = \frac{g_0^T g_0}{g_0^T A(-H_0^{-1}g_0)}\text{ ... but let's use the general formula.}$$ Actually, for exact line search: $\alpha = -\frac{g_0^T d_0}{d_0^T A d_0}$. $$g_0^T d_0 = (6)(-6) + (-2)(2) = -36 - 4 = -40$$ $$Ad_0 = \begin{bmatrix}4&-2\\-2&2\end{bmatrix}\begin{bmatrix}-6\\2\end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix}-24-4\\12+4\end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix}-28\\16\end{bmatrix}$$ $$d_0^T A d_0 = (-6)(-28) + (2)(16) = 168 + 32 = 200$$ $$\alpha_0 = \frac{-(-40)}{200} = \frac{40}{200} = \frac{1}{5}$$
Step 4: Update the point
$$x_1 = x_0 + \alpha_0 d_0 = \begin{bmatrix}2\\1\end{bmatrix} + \frac{1}{5}\begin{bmatrix}-6\\2\end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix}2-6/5\\1+2/5\end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix}4/5\\7/5\end{bmatrix}$$
Step 5: Compute $s_0$ and $y_0$
$$s_0 = x_1 - x_0 = \alpha_0 d_0 = \frac{1}{5}\begin{bmatrix}-6\\2\end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix}-6/5\\2/5\end{bmatrix}$$ $$g_1 = \nabla f(x_1) = \begin{bmatrix}4(4/5) - 2(7/5)\\2(7/5) - 2(4/5)\end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix}16/5 - 14/5\\14/5 - 8/5\end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix}2/5\\6/5\end{bmatrix}$$ $$y_0 = g_1 - g_0 = \begin{bmatrix}2/5\\6/5\end{bmatrix} - \begin{bmatrix}6\\-2\end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix}2/5 - 30/5\\6/5 + 10/5\end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix}-28/5\\16/5\end{bmatrix}$$
Step 6: Curvature condition check
$$s_0^T y_0 = \left(-\frac{6}{5}\right)\left(-\frac{28}{5}\right) + \left(\frac{2}{5}\right)\left(\frac{16}{5}\right) = \frac{168}{25} + \frac{32}{25} = \frac{200}{25} = 8$$

$s_0^T y_0 = 8 \gt 0$. Curvature condition satisfied; proceed with BFGS update.

Step 7: BFGS inverse Hessian update
$\rho_0 = \frac{1}{s_0^T y_0} = \frac{1}{8}$ The BFGS update formula: $$H_1 = (I - \rho_0 s_0 y_0^T)H_0(I - \rho_0 y_0 s_0^T) + \rho_0 s_0 s_0^T$$ Compute $\rho_0 s_0 y_0^T$: $$\rho_0 s_0 y_0^T = \frac{1}{8}\begin{bmatrix}-6/5\\2/5\end{bmatrix}\begin{bmatrix}-28/5 & 16/5\end{bmatrix} = \frac{1}{8}\begin{bmatrix}168/25 & -96/25\\-56/25 & 32/25\end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix}21/25 & -12/25\\-7/25 & 4/25\end{bmatrix}$$ $$V_0 = I - \rho_0 s_0 y_0^T = \begin{bmatrix}1-21/25 & 12/25\\7/25 & 1-4/25\end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix}4/25 & 12/25\\7/25 & 21/25\end{bmatrix}$$ Since $H_0 = I$: $$V_0 H_0 V_0^T = V_0 V_0^T$$ $$V_0 V_0^T = \begin{bmatrix}4/25 & 12/25\\7/25 & 21/25\end{bmatrix}\begin{bmatrix}4/25 & 7/25\\12/25 & 21/25\end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix}(16+144)/625 & (28+252)/625\\(28+252)/625 & (49+441)/625\end{bmatrix}$$ $$= \begin{bmatrix}160/625 & 280/625\\280/625 & 490/625\end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix}32/125 & 56/125\\56/125 & 98/125\end{bmatrix}$$ $$\rho_0 s_0 s_0^T = \frac{1}{8}\begin{bmatrix}36/25 & -12/25\\-12/25 & 4/25\end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix}9/50 & -3/50\\-3/50 & 1/50\end{bmatrix}$$ Converting to common denominator (/250): $$H_1 = \begin{bmatrix}64/250 + 45/250 & 112/250 - 15/250\\112/250 - 15/250 & 196/250 + 5/250\end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix}109/250 & 97/250\\97/250 & 201/250\end{bmatrix}$$
$$H_1 = \frac{1}{250}\begin{bmatrix}109 & 97\\97 & 201\end{bmatrix}$$

For reference, the true inverse Hessian is $A^{-1} = \frac{1}{4}\begin{bmatrix}2&2\\2&4\end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix}0.5&0.5\\0.5&1.0\end{bmatrix}$. Our $H_1 \approx \begin{bmatrix}0.436&0.388\\0.388&0.804\end{bmatrix}$; getting closer! A second BFGS step on a quadratic would give the exact inverse Hessian.

Part F: Newton for Nonlinear Systems

Core method from Page 14. Mirrors Quiz 7 format exactly.

Problem: Solve the system $$r(x) = \begin{bmatrix} x_1^2 + x_2 - 3 \\ x_1 + x_2^2 - 5 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 0 \\ 0 \end{bmatrix}$$ starting from $x_0 = \begin{bmatrix}1\\1\end{bmatrix}$. Perform one Newton step.

Full Worked Solution

Step 1: Evaluate the residual at $x_0$
$$r(x_0) = \begin{bmatrix}1 + 1 - 3\\1 + 1 - 5\end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix}-1\\-3\end{bmatrix}$$
Step 2: Compute the Jacobian at $x_0$
The Jacobian $J(x)$ is the matrix of partial derivatives of the residual components (Page 02): $$J(x) = \begin{bmatrix} \partial r_1/\partial x_1 & \partial r_1/\partial x_2 \\ \partial r_2/\partial x_1 & \partial r_2/\partial x_2 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 2x_1 & 1 \\ 1 & 2x_2 \end{bmatrix}$$ $$J(x_0) = \begin{bmatrix}2&1\\1&2\end{bmatrix}$$
Step 3: Solve $J(x_0)\, p_0 = -r(x_0)$
$$\begin{bmatrix}2&1\\1&2\end{bmatrix}\begin{bmatrix}p_1\\p_2\end{bmatrix} = -\begin{bmatrix}-1\\-3\end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix}1\\3\end{bmatrix}$$ Using the $2 \times 2$ inverse: $\det(J) = 4 - 1 = 3$. $$J^{-1} = \frac{1}{3}\begin{bmatrix}2&-1\\-1&2\end{bmatrix}$$ $$p_0 = J^{-1}\begin{bmatrix}1\\3\end{bmatrix} = \frac{1}{3}\begin{bmatrix}2-3\\-1+6\end{bmatrix} = \frac{1}{3}\begin{bmatrix}-1\\5\end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix}-1/3\\5/3\end{bmatrix}$$
Step 4: Update the point
$$x_1 = x_0 + p_0 = \begin{bmatrix}1\\1\end{bmatrix} + \begin{bmatrix}-1/3\\5/3\end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix}2/3\\8/3\end{bmatrix}$$
$$x_1 = \begin{bmatrix}2/3\\8/3\end{bmatrix} \approx \begin{bmatrix}0.667\\2.667\end{bmatrix}$$
Step 5: Verify; is the residual smaller?
$$r(x_1) = \begin{bmatrix}(2/3)^2 + 8/3 - 3\\2/3 + (8/3)^2 - 5\end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix}4/9 + 8/3 - 3\\2/3 + 64/9 - 5\end{bmatrix}$$ $$= \begin{bmatrix}4/9 + 24/9 - 27/9\\6/9 + 64/9 - 45/9\end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix}1/9\\25/9\end{bmatrix}$$ $$\|r(x_0)\| = \sqrt{1 + 9} = \sqrt{10} \approx 3.162$$ $$\|r(x_1)\| = \sqrt{1/81 + 625/81} = \sqrt{626/81} \approx 2.780$$

Residual norm decreased from 3.162 to 2.780. Newton is making progress. The convergence will speed up dramatically as we get closer to the root (quadratic convergence kicks in).

Part G: KKT Conditions & Constrained Optimization

Core method from Page 15. Mirrors Quiz 8 format exactly.

Problem: Solve $$\min\; f(x_1, x_2) = x_1^2 + x_2^2$$ $$\text{subject to: } \quad x_1 + x_2 \geq 4, \quad x_1 \geq 0, \quad x_2 \geq 0$$

Full Worked Solution

Step 1: Convert to standard form (inequality constraints $c_i(x) \geq 0$)
We have three constraints:
  • $c_1(x) = x_1 + x_2 - 4 \geq 0$
  • $c_2(x) = x_1 \geq 0$
  • $c_3(x) = x_2 \geq 0$
Step 2: Write the Lagrangian
$$\mathcal{L}(x, \lambda) = f(x) - \lambda_1 c_1(x) - \lambda_2 c_2(x) - \lambda_3 c_3(x)$$ $$= x_1^2 + x_2^2 - \lambda_1(x_1 + x_2 - 4) - \lambda_2 x_1 - \lambda_3 x_2$$
Step 3: Write the KKT conditions
Stationarity ($\nabla_x \mathcal{L} = 0$): $$\frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial x_1} = 2x_1 - \lambda_1 - \lambda_2 = 0 \quad \Rightarrow \quad 2x_1 = \lambda_1 + \lambda_2 \quad \text{...(i)}$$ $$\frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial x_2} = 2x_2 - \lambda_1 - \lambda_3 = 0 \quad \Rightarrow \quad 2x_2 = \lambda_1 + \lambda_3 \quad \text{...(ii)}$$ Primal feasibility: $x_1 + x_2 \geq 4$, $x_1 \geq 0$, $x_2 \geq 0$ Dual feasibility: $\lambda_1 \geq 0$, $\lambda_2 \geq 0$, $\lambda_3 \geq 0$ Complementary slackness: $$\lambda_1(x_1 + x_2 - 4) = 0 \quad \text{...(iii)}$$ $$\lambda_2 x_1 = 0 \quad \text{...(iv)}$$ $$\lambda_3 x_2 = 0 \quad \text{...(v)}$$
Step 4: Enumerate cases using complementary slackness

Each $\lambda_i$ is either 0 or its constraint is active (equality). We check cases systematically.

Case 1: Only $c_1$ active ($\lambda_1 \gt 0$, $\lambda_2 = 0$, $\lambda_3 = 0$)

From (i): $2x_1 = \lambda_1$

From (ii): $2x_2 = \lambda_1$

So $x_1 = x_2$.

From $c_1$ active: $x_1 + x_2 = 4$, so $x_1 = x_2 = 2$.

Then $\lambda_1 = 2(2) = 4 \gt 0$.

Check primal feasibility: $x_1 = 2 \gt 0$, $x_2 = 2 \gt 0$.

KKT point: $x^* = (2, 2)$, $\lambda_1 = 4$, $\lambda_2 = \lambda_3 = 0$. All conditions satisfied!

Case 2: Only $c_2$ active ($\lambda_1 = 0$, $\lambda_2 \gt 0$, $\lambda_3 = 0$)

From (iv): $x_1 = 0$.

From (i): $0 = \lambda_2$, but we assumed $\lambda_2 \gt 0$. Contradiction.

No solution.

Case 3: $c_1$ and $c_2$ active ($\lambda_1 \gt 0$, $\lambda_2 \gt 0$, $\lambda_3 = 0$)

$x_1 = 0$ and $x_1 + x_2 = 4$, so $x_2 = 4$.

From (ii): $2(4) = \lambda_1$, so $\lambda_1 = 8$.

From (i): $0 = 8 + \lambda_2$, so $\lambda_2 = -8 \lt 0$.

Violates dual feasibility!

Case 4: $c_1$ and $c_3$ active (by symmetry with Case 3)

$x_2 = 0$, $x_1 = 4$.

From (i): $8 = \lambda_1 + \lambda_2$

From (ii): $0 = \lambda_1 + \lambda_3$, so $\lambda_3 = -\lambda_1$.

Since $\lambda_1 \geq 0$ and $\lambda_3 \geq 0$, we need $\lambda_1 = 0$ and $\lambda_3 = 0$. Then $\lambda_2 = 8$.

Check: if $\lambda_1 = 0$, from (i) $8 = \lambda_2$, from (ii) $0 = \lambda_3$. Check $c_1$: $4 + 0 - 4 = 0 \geq 0$. This works, but $(4, 0)$ gives $f = 16$, which is worse than $(2,2)$ giving $f = 8$.

By symmetry, $(0, 4)$ gives $f = 16$ as well.

Step 5: Identify the optimal solution
Comparing KKT points:
  • $(2, 2)$: $f = 4 + 4 = 8$
  • $(4, 0)$: $f = 16$
  • $(0, 4)$: $f = 16$
The optimal solution is $x^* = (2, 2)$ with $f^* = 8$ and multiplier $\lambda_1 = 4$.

Geometric check: we are minimizing distance from origin subject to being on or above the line $x_1 + x_2 = 4$ in the first quadrant. The closest point on that line to the origin is $(2,2)$ (by symmetry). The multiplier $\lambda_1 = 4 \gt 0$ tells us the constraint is active (binding) and "costs" us; if we could relax it, we would move closer to the origin.

Part H: Logistic Regression Gradient Step

Classification method from Page 24. Uses gradient descent from Page 06.

Problem: Given data matrix $A = \begin{bmatrix}1 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 \\ 1 & 1\end{bmatrix}$ (rows are data points $x_i^T$), labels $y = \begin{bmatrix}1 \\ -1 \\ 1\end{bmatrix}$, current weights $\theta = \begin{bmatrix}0 \\ 0\end{bmatrix}$, learning rate $\eta = 0.5$. Compute one gradient descent step on the logistic loss: $$f(\theta) = \frac{1}{3}\sum_{i=1}^{3} \log(1 + e^{-y_i \theta^T x_i})$$

Full Worked Solution

Step 1: Compute the margin $z_i = y_i \theta^T x_i$ for each point
At $\theta = \begin{bmatrix}0\\0\end{bmatrix}$, the dot product $\theta^T x_i = 0$ for every point: $$z_1 = y_1 \theta^T x_1 = (1)(0) = 0$$ $$z_2 = y_2 \theta^T x_2 = (-1)(0) = 0$$ $$z_3 = y_3 \theta^T x_3 = (1)(0) = 0$$
Step 2: Write the gradient formula and evaluate the common factor
The gradient of the logistic loss is: $$\nabla f(\theta) = -\frac{1}{3}\sum_{i=1}^{3} \frac{y_i x_i}{1 + e^{z_i}}$$ Since all $z_i = 0$ and $e^0 = 1$, each denominator is $1 + 1 = 2$: $$\frac{1}{1 + e^{z_i}} = \frac{1}{2} \quad \text{for all } i$$
Step 3: Compute the gradient
$$\nabla f = -\frac{1}{3} \cdot \frac{1}{2}\left[y_1 x_1 + y_2 x_2 + y_3 x_3\right]$$ $$= -\frac{1}{6}\left[(1)\begin{bmatrix}1\\0\end{bmatrix} + (-1)\begin{bmatrix}0\\1\end{bmatrix} + (1)\begin{bmatrix}1\\1\end{bmatrix}\right]$$ $$= -\frac{1}{6}\left[\begin{bmatrix}1\\0\end{bmatrix} + \begin{bmatrix}0\\-1\end{bmatrix} + \begin{bmatrix}1\\1\end{bmatrix}\right] = -\frac{1}{6}\begin{bmatrix}2\\0\end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix}-1/3\\0\end{bmatrix}$$
Step 4: Update the weights
$$\theta_1 = \theta_0 - \eta \nabla f = \begin{bmatrix}0\\0\end{bmatrix} - 0.5\begin{bmatrix}-1/3\\0\end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix}0 + 1/6\\0\end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix}1/6\\0\end{bmatrix}$$
$$\theta_1 = \begin{bmatrix}1/6\\0\end{bmatrix} \approx \begin{bmatrix}0.167\\0\end{bmatrix}$$
Step 5: Verify; the update makes sense

The first component moved positive, which makes sense: $x_1 = (1,0)$ has label $+1$ and $x_3 = (1,1)$ has label $+1$; both have a positive first component. The second component stayed at zero because the positive label point $x_3$ and negative label point $x_2$ cancel out in that coordinate. The decision boundary $\theta^T x = 0$ is now the line $x_1 = 0$ (vertical), which correctly puts $x_1$ and $x_3$ on the positive side.

Part I: SVM Dual Setup

Constrained optimization from Page 25. Uses KKT from Page 15.

Problem: Given 3 data points in 2D:
  • $x_1 = (1,1)$, $y_1 = +1$
  • $x_2 = (-1,-1)$, $y_2 = -1$
  • $x_3 = (2,0)$, $y_3 = +1$
Write out the Wolfe dual objective and identify all constraints.

Full Worked Solution

Step 1: Compute all dot products $x_i^T x_j$
$$x_1^T x_1 = 1(1) + 1(1) = 2$$ $$x_1^T x_2 = 1(-1) + 1(-1) = -2$$ $$x_1^T x_3 = 1(2) + 1(0) = 2$$ $$x_2^T x_2 = (-1)(-1) + (-1)(-1) = 2$$ $$x_2^T x_3 = (-1)(2) + (-1)(0) = -2$$ $$x_3^T x_3 = 2(2) + 0(0) = 4$$
Step 2: Compute the $y_i y_j (x_i^T x_j)$ matrix entries
The dual objective involves $y_i y_j (x_i^T x_j)$. Using $y_1 = +1, y_2 = -1, y_3 = +1$:
$(i,j)$$y_i y_j$$x_i^T x_j$$y_i y_j (x_i^T x_j)$
(1,1)$+1$$2$$2$
(1,2)$-1$$-2$$2$
(1,3)$+1$$2$$2$
(2,2)$+1$$2$$2$
(2,3)$-1$$-2$$2$
(3,3)$+1$$4$$4$
Step 3: Write the dual objective
The Wolfe dual is $\max_\alpha Q(\alpha) = \sum_i \alpha_i - \frac{1}{2}\sum_i\sum_j \alpha_i \alpha_j y_i y_j (x_i^T x_j)$. Expanding the double sum (using symmetry: cross terms appear twice): $$Q(\alpha) = \alpha_1 + \alpha_2 + \alpha_3 - \frac{1}{2}\Big[2\alpha_1^2 + 2\alpha_2^2 + 4\alpha_3^2 + 2(2)\alpha_1\alpha_2 + 2(2)\alpha_1\alpha_3 + 2(2)\alpha_2\alpha_3\Big]$$ $$= \alpha_1 + \alpha_2 + \alpha_3 - \frac{1}{2}\Big[2\alpha_1^2 + 2\alpha_2^2 + 4\alpha_3^2 + 4\alpha_1\alpha_2 + 4\alpha_1\alpha_3 + 4\alpha_2\alpha_3\Big]$$
$$Q(\alpha) = \alpha_1 + \alpha_2 + \alpha_3 - \alpha_1^2 - \alpha_2^2 - 2\alpha_3^2 - 2\alpha_1\alpha_2 - 2\alpha_1\alpha_3 - 2\alpha_2\alpha_3$$
Step 4: Write the constraints
Non-negativity: $\alpha_1 \geq 0, \; \alpha_2 \geq 0, \; \alpha_3 \geq 0$ Equality constraint (from $\sum \alpha_i y_i = 0$): $$\alpha_1 y_1 + \alpha_2 y_2 + \alpha_3 y_3 = 0$$ $$(+1)\alpha_1 + (-1)\alpha_2 + (+1)\alpha_3 = 0$$
$$\alpha_1 - \alpha_2 + \alpha_3 = 0, \qquad \alpha_i \geq 0$$

The equality constraint says $\alpha_2 = \alpha_1 + \alpha_3$: the total "weight" on the negative class must equal the total weight on the positive class. This ensures $\sum \alpha_i y_i = 0$, which came from $\partial L / \partial b = 0$ in the stationarity conditions.

Part J: MLP Forward and Backward Pass

Neural network from Page 26. Uses gradient descent from Page 06.

Problem: Perform one gradient descent step for a simple MLP with:
  • Input $x = [1, \; 0.5]^T$, target $y = 1$
  • $W_1 = \begin{bmatrix}0.2 & 0.3\\0.1 & 0.4\end{bmatrix}$, $W_2 = [0.5, \; 0.6]$
  • All biases $= 0$, linear activation, learning rate $\eta = 0.1$

Full Worked Solution

Step 1: Forward pass; compute hidden layer
$$z^{(1)} = W_1 x = \begin{bmatrix}0.2 & 0.3\\0.1 & 0.4\end{bmatrix}\begin{bmatrix}1\\0.5\end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix}0.2(1) + 0.3(0.5)\\0.1(1) + 0.4(0.5)\end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix}0.35\\0.3\end{bmatrix}$$ Since activation is linear: $a^{(1)} = z^{(1)} = \begin{bmatrix}0.35\\0.3\end{bmatrix}$
Step 2: Forward pass; compute output
$$\hat{y} = W_2 a^{(1)} = [0.5, \; 0.6]\begin{bmatrix}0.35\\0.3\end{bmatrix} = 0.5(0.35) + 0.6(0.3) = 0.175 + 0.18 = 0.355$$
Step 3: Compute the loss
$$J = \frac{1}{2}(y - \hat{y})^2 = \frac{1}{2}(1 - 0.355)^2 = \frac{1}{2}(0.645)^2 = \frac{1}{2}(0.416025) \approx 0.208$$
Step 4: Backward pass; output error
$$\delta^{(2)} = -(y - \hat{y}) = -(1 - 0.355) = -0.645$$
Step 5: Gradient with respect to $W_2$
$$\nabla_{W_2} J = \delta^{(2)} \cdot (a^{(1)})^T = (-0.645) \cdot [0.35, \; 0.3] = [-0.2258, \; -0.1935]$$
Step 6: Backward pass; propagate error to hidden layer
$$W_2^T \delta^{(2)} = \begin{bmatrix}0.5\\0.6\end{bmatrix}(-0.645) = \begin{bmatrix}-0.3225\\-0.387\end{bmatrix}$$ Since activation is linear ($\sigma'(z) = 1$), the hidden error is just $W_2^T \delta^{(2)}$.
Step 7: Gradient with respect to $W_1$
$$\nabla_{W_1} J = [W_2^T \delta^{(2)}] \cdot x^T = \begin{bmatrix}-0.3225\\-0.387\end{bmatrix}[1, \; 0.5] = \begin{bmatrix}-0.3225 & -0.1613\\-0.387 & -0.1935\end{bmatrix}$$
Step 8: Update $W_2$
$$W_2^{\text{new}} = W_2 - \eta \nabla_{W_2} J = [0.5, \; 0.6] - 0.1[-0.2258, \; -0.1935]$$ $$= [0.5 + 0.0226, \; 0.6 + 0.0194] = [0.5226, \; 0.6194]$$
$$W_2^{\text{new}} = [0.5226, \; 0.6194]$$
Step 9: Update $W_1$
$$W_1^{\text{new}} = W_1 - \eta \nabla_{W_1} J = \begin{bmatrix}0.2 & 0.3\\0.1 & 0.4\end{bmatrix} - 0.1\begin{bmatrix}-0.3225 & -0.1613\\-0.387 & -0.1935\end{bmatrix}$$ $$= \begin{bmatrix}0.2 + 0.0323 & 0.3 + 0.0161\\0.1 + 0.0387 & 0.4 + 0.0194\end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix}0.2323 & 0.3161\\0.1387 & 0.4194\end{bmatrix}$$
$$W_1^{\text{new}} = \begin{bmatrix}0.2323 & 0.3161\\0.1387 & 0.4194\end{bmatrix}$$
Step 10: Verify; weights moved in the right direction

All weights increased because the network under-predicted ($\hat{y} = 0.355 \lt 1 = y$). The gradients are negative (pointing toward decreasing loss), so subtracting $\eta \cdot \nabla$ makes each weight larger, which increases $\hat{y}$ toward the target. The output weights $W_2$ changed more where the hidden activation was larger ($a_1 = 0.35 \gt a_2 = 0.3$), matching the chain rule weighting.

Part K: NMF Multiplicative Update

Core method from Page 22. Tests understanding of element-wise matrix operations.

Problem: Given $V = \begin{bmatrix} 4 & 6 \\ 2 & 3 \end{bmatrix}$, initial factors $W^{(0)} = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix}$, $H^{(0)} = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 1 \end{bmatrix}$ (rank-1 factorization). Perform one iteration of the Lee-Seung multiplicative update.

Full Worked Solution

Step 1: Current approximation
$$W^{(0)} H^{(0)} = \begin{bmatrix}1\\1\end{bmatrix}\begin{bmatrix}1 & 1\end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix}1 & 1\\1 & 1\end{bmatrix}$$ Current error: $\|V - WH\|_F^2 = (4-1)^2 + (6-1)^2 + (2-1)^2 + (3-1)^2 = 9+25+1+4 = 39$
Step 2: Update $H$ (coefficients)
The multiplicative update rule for $H$: $$H_{ij} \leftarrow H_{ij} \frac{(W^T V)_{ij}}{(W^T W H)_{ij}}$$ Compute numerator $W^T V$: $$W^T V = \begin{bmatrix}1 & 1\end{bmatrix}\begin{bmatrix}4 & 6\\2 & 3\end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix}6 & 9\end{bmatrix}$$ Compute denominator $W^T W H$: $$W^T W = \begin{bmatrix}1 & 1\end{bmatrix}\begin{bmatrix}1\\1\end{bmatrix} = 2$$ $$W^T W H = 2 \cdot \begin{bmatrix}1 & 1\end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix}2 & 2\end{bmatrix}$$ Apply element-wise: $$H^{(1)} = H^{(0)} \odot \frac{W^T V}{W^T W H} = \begin{bmatrix}1 & 1\end{bmatrix} \odot \frac{\begin{bmatrix}6 & 9\end{bmatrix}}{\begin{bmatrix}2 & 2\end{bmatrix}} = \begin{bmatrix}1 \cdot 3 & 1 \cdot 4.5\end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix}3 & 4.5\end{bmatrix}$$
Step 3: Update $W$ (basis)
The multiplicative update rule for $W$: $$W_{ij} \leftarrow W_{ij} \frac{(V H^T)_{ij}}{(W H H^T)_{ij}}$$ Using the NEW $H^{(1)} = [3, 4.5]$: Compute numerator $V (H^{(1)})^T$: $$V (H^{(1)})^T = \begin{bmatrix}4 & 6\\2 & 3\end{bmatrix}\begin{bmatrix}3\\4.5\end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix}12+27\\6+13.5\end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix}39\\19.5\end{bmatrix}$$ Compute denominator $W^{(0)} H^{(1)} (H^{(1)})^T$: $$H^{(1)} (H^{(1)})^T = [3, 4.5][3, 4.5]^T = 9 + 20.25 = 29.25$$ $$W^{(0)} \cdot 29.25 = \begin{bmatrix}29.25\\29.25\end{bmatrix}$$ Apply element-wise: $$W^{(1)} = W^{(0)} \odot \frac{V H^T}{W H H^T} = \begin{bmatrix}1\\1\end{bmatrix} \odot \frac{\begin{bmatrix}39\\19.5\end{bmatrix}}{\begin{bmatrix}29.25\\29.25\end{bmatrix}} = \begin{bmatrix}39/29.25\\19.5/29.25\end{bmatrix} \approx \begin{bmatrix}1.333\\0.667\end{bmatrix}$$
Step 4: Check new approximation
$$W^{(1)} H^{(1)} = \begin{bmatrix}1.333\\0.667\end{bmatrix}\begin{bmatrix}3 & 4.5\end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix}4.0 & 6.0\\2.0 & 3.0\end{bmatrix}$$
The reconstruction after one iteration is $W^{(1)}H^{(1)} = \begin{bmatrix}4 & 6\\2 & 3\end{bmatrix} = V$ exactly! The error $\|V - WH\|_F^2 = 0$.

This rank-1 matrix happened to be exactly recoverable because $V$ itself has rank 1: $V = \begin{bmatrix}2\\1\end{bmatrix}\begin{bmatrix}2 & 3\end{bmatrix}$. In general, convergence takes many iterations.
