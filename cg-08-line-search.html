Line Search Methods

You know which direction to walk; now figure out how far

Part A: The Line Search Idea

Physical analogy; "Which direction" is settled, now decide "how far."

Imagine you are hiking and you have already decided which direction to walk. Maybe you decided to walk north (gradient descent chose the steepest downhill direction from Page 06), or maybe a more sophisticated method chose a smarter direction (Newton from Page 07, or CG from Page 10). Either way, the direction $p_k$ is fixed.

Now the question is: how far should you walk in that direction? Take a tiny step? A huge leap? If you step too little, you waste time; you could have made more progress. If you step too far, you might overshoot the bottom and end up climbing uphill on the other side.

Finding the right step size $\alpha_k$ is the line search problem. It is called "line search" because you are searching along a line (the ray starting at $x_k$ pointing in direction $p_k$) for the best point. You have reduced the multi-dimensional optimization problem to a one-dimensional search along this line.

The beautiful insight: you do not need to find the PERFECT step size. You just need a step size that is "good enough"; one that guarantees sufficient progress without being too expensive to compute. That is what the Armijo and Wolfe conditions are about.
The Line Search Framework

Given: current point $x_k$ and search direction $p_k$ (a descent direction, meaning $\nabla f(x_k)^T p_k \lt 0$).

Find: step size $\alpha_k \gt 0$.

Update: $x_{k+1} = x_k + \alpha_k p_k$.

To analyze the step size, we define the one-dimensional slice:

$$\phi(\alpha) = f(x_k + \alpha p_k)$$
$\phi(\alpha)$
the function value as you walk a distance $\alpha$ in direction $p_k$. This converts the multi-dimensional problem into a 1D function of the single variable $\alpha$.
$\alpha = 0$
$\phi(0) = f(x_k)$, your current function value
$\alpha \gt 0$
walking forward along $p_k$
$\phi'(\alpha)$
$= \nabla f(x_k + \alpha p_k)^T p_k$; the slope of $\phi$ at step size $\alpha$. By the chain rule.
$\phi'(0)$
$= \nabla f(x_k)^T p_k \lt 0$; the initial slope is negative because $p_k$ is a descent direction. This guarantees $\phi$ is initially decreasing.
Key insight: We almost never compute the exact minimizer $\alpha^*$ of $\phi(\alpha)$. That would require solving a 1D optimization problem exactly, which can be expensive and is usually unnecessary. Instead, we use inexact line search conditions that guarantee "enough progress" without too much computation. The two most important are the Armijo condition and the Wolfe conditions.

Part B: Armijo Condition (Sufficient Decrease)

Physical analogy; The progress bar.

Imagine you have a progress bar for your descent. The progress bar sets a minimum standard: "I expect the function to decrease by at least THIS much." The expected decrease is based on a simple linear prediction: if the initial slope is $\phi'(0)$ and you walk a distance $\alpha$, a naive linear model predicts the function value drops by $|\phi'(0)| \cdot \alpha$. But you do not demand the full predicted decrease; that would be too strict. You only demand a fraction $c_1$ of the predicted decrease, where $c_1$ is a small number like $10^{-4}$.

If the actual decrease meets or exceeds this modest bar, the step passes. If not, the step was too ambitious; you probably overshot a minimum and ended up too high on the other side. In that case, shrink the step size and try again.

Why such a low bar ($c_1 = 10^{-4}$)? Because the Armijo condition's job is just to prevent catastrophically bad steps. It is saying "at least make some progress." The curvature condition (Part C) will handle the other extreme of steps that are too short.
The Armijo Condition (Sufficient Decrease) $$f(x_k + \alpha p_k) \leq f(x_k) + c_1 \alpha \nabla f(x_k)^T p_k$$

Equivalently, in terms of $\phi$:

$$\phi(\alpha) \leq \phi(0) + c_1 \alpha \phi'(0)$$
Left side: $f(x_k + \alpha p_k)$
the actual function value after taking a step of size $\alpha$
$f(x_k)$
the current function value (before the step)
$c_1$
a small constant, typically $c_1 = 10^{-4}$. It controls how much decrease we demand. Must satisfy $0 \lt c_1 \lt 1$.
$\alpha$
the candidate step size we are testing
$\nabla f(x_k)^T p_k$
the directional derivative at $x_k$ in direction $p_k$. This is negative because $p_k$ is a descent direction. So $c_1 \alpha \nabla f(x_k)^T p_k$ is a negative number, making the right side less than $f(x_k)$.
$c_1 \alpha \nabla f(x_k)^T p_k$
the "minimum expected decrease." It is a fraction $c_1$ of what the linear model predicts.

Why does Armijo alone have a problem?

The Armijo condition can be satisfied by extremely tiny step sizes. If $\alpha$ is tiny, then $\phi(\alpha) \approx \phi(0) + \alpha \phi'(0)$, which is less than $\phi(0) + c_1 \alpha \phi'(0)$ because $\phi'(0) \lt 0$ and $c_1 \lt 1$. So $\alpha = 10^{-100}$ would pass Armijo!

Tiny steps make progress, technically, but they waste iterations. We need a second condition to prevent steps that are too short. That is the curvature condition (Wolfe).

Part C: Wolfe Conditions

Physical analogy; "Don't stop on a steep downhill slope."

The Armijo condition says "you must make enough progress"; it prevents steps that are too long (overshoot). But it allows steps that are too short. The curvature condition adds: "don't stop while you're still on a steep downhill slope; keep going until the slope flattens out."

Think of it this way. You are walking downhill. Armijo says "make sure you've actually gone downhill." Wolfe adds "don't stop while the ground is still steeply sloping; you're leaving easy progress on the table." Together, they bracket the step size into a "Goldilocks zone"; not too big, not too small.
The Wolfe Conditions

A step size $\alpha_k$ satisfies the Wolfe conditions if BOTH hold:

1. Armijo (Sufficient Decrease):

$$f(x_k + \alpha_k p_k) \leq f(x_k) + c_1 \alpha_k \nabla f(x_k)^T p_k$$

2. Curvature Condition:

$$\nabla f(x_k + \alpha_k p_k)^T p_k \geq c_2 \, \nabla f(x_k)^T p_k$$

where $0 \lt c_1 \lt c_2 \lt 1$. Typical values: $c_1 = 10^{-4}$, $c_2 = 0.9$ (for Newton/quasi-Newton) or $c_2 = 0.1$ (for CG).

Left side of (2): $\nabla f(x_k + \alpha_k p_k)^T p_k$
the directional derivative (slope of $\phi$) at the new point $x_k + \alpha_k p_k$. This is $\phi'(\alpha_k)$.
Right side of (2): $c_2 \, \nabla f(x_k)^T p_k$
a fraction $c_2$ of the initial slope $\phi'(0)$. Since $\phi'(0) \lt 0$, this is a negative number.
The inequality $\phi'(\alpha_k) \geq c_2 \phi'(0)$
says the slope at the new point must be less steep (less negative) than $c_2$ times the initial slope. In other words: the slope must have flattened out at least somewhat.
Reading the curvature condition: Both sides are negative (for a descent direction before reaching a minimum). The right side is more negative (since $c_2 \lt 1$ and we are multiplying a negative number by a fraction less than 1, we get a number closer to zero than the original). So the condition says: the new slope $\phi'(\alpha)$ must be "closer to zero or positive" compared to $c_2 \phi'(0)$. Geometrically: you must have walked far enough that the slope has flattened.
Strong Wolfe Conditions

Replace the curvature condition with an absolute value version:

1. Armijo (same):

$$f(x_k + \alpha_k p_k) \leq f(x_k) + c_1 \alpha_k \nabla f(x_k)^T p_k$$

2. Strong Curvature Condition:

$$\bigl|\nabla f(x_k + \alpha_k p_k)^T p_k\bigr| \leq c_2 \bigl|\nabla f(x_k)^T p_k\bigr|$$
$|\phi'(\alpha_k)| \leq c_2 |\phi'(0)|$
The absolute value of the slope at the new point must be small relative to the initial slope. This prevents accepting a point where the slope is steep in EITHER direction (too steep downhill OR too steep uphill). The regular Wolfe condition allows steep uphill slopes.

Why does BFGS need the Strong Wolfe conditions?

The BFGS update formula (Page 12) requires the curvature condition $s_k^T y_k \gt 0$, where $s_k = x_{k+1} - x_k$ and $y_k = \nabla f(x_{k+1}) - \nabla f(x_k)$.

Writing this out: $s_k^T y_k = \alpha_k p_k^T (\nabla f(x_{k+1}) - \nabla f(x_k)) = \alpha_k (\phi'(\alpha_k) - \phi'(0))$. Since $\phi'(0) \lt 0$, the Wolfe curvature condition $\phi'(\alpha_k) \geq c_2 \phi'(0)$ (with $c_2 \lt 1$) implies $\phi'(\alpha_k) \gt \phi'(0)$, which gives $s_k^T y_k \gt 0$.

The strong Wolfe condition additionally prevents $\phi'(\alpha_k)$ from being too large positive, which keeps the BFGS update well-behaved.

Goldstein Conditions

The Goldstein conditions place symmetric upper and lower bounds on the function value after a step:

$$f(x_k) + (1-c)\alpha_k \nabla f_k^T p_k \leq f(x_k + \alpha_k p_k) \leq f(x_k) + c\,\alpha_k \nabla f_k^T p_k, \quad 0 \lt c \lt \frac{1}{2}$$
Upper bound (right inequality)
This is exactly the Armijo condition: the function must decrease by at least a fraction $c$ of the linear prediction. Prevents steps that are too long (insufficient decrease).
Lower bound (left inequality)
Prevents steps that are too short. It says: the function cannot decrease by more than a fraction $(1-c)$ of the linear prediction. If you walk so little that the function drops almost as much as the tangent line predicts, you should keep walking; there is easy progress still on the table.
$c$
Typically $c = 10^{-4}$. Must satisfy $0 \lt c \lt 1/2$ so that the upper and lower bounds are compatible (the interval between the two lines is nonempty).
$\nabla f_k^T p_k$
The directional derivative at $x_k$, which is negative because $p_k$ is a descent direction. So $(1-c)\alpha_k \nabla f_k^T p_k$ is more negative than $c\,\alpha_k \nabla f_k^T p_k$ (since $1-c \gt c$ when $c \lt 1/2$), giving a valid interval.

Goldstein vs. Wolfe: Drawback

The Goldstein and Wolfe conditions have similar convergence properties for general line search methods. However, Goldstein has a key drawback: the lower bound is a straight line, and the true function $\phi(\alpha)$ may dip below this line at the minimizer $\alpha^*$. This means the Goldstein conditions can exclude all minimizers of $\phi(\alpha)$, forcing the accepted step to be away from the optimal step length.

More critically, Goldstein is less suitable for quasi-Newton methods because it cannot guarantee $s_k^T y_k \gt 0$ (the curvature condition needed by BFGS; see Page 12). The Wolfe curvature condition directly ensures this property, which is why Wolfe (not Goldstein) is standard for BFGS and L-BFGS.

Quiz 3 Connection: Comparing All Four Conditions

Quiz 3 asked you to compare Armijo, Goldstein, Wolfe, and Strong Wolfe on logistic regression. Key takeaways:

  • Armijo alone: Allows too-short steps. Simple to implement. Used in backtracking.
  • Goldstein: Symmetric bounds. Simple. But cannot guarantee $s_k^T y_k \gt 0$ for BFGS.
  • Wolfe: Armijo + curvature. Guarantees $s_k^T y_k \gt 0$. Standard for quasi-Newton.
  • Strong Wolfe: Wolfe with absolute value. Best for BFGS. Slightly harder to implement.

Part D: Backtracking Line Search Algorithm

Physical analogy; "Start optimistic, shrink if needed."

Backtracking is the simplest practical line search algorithm. The idea: start with an ambitious step size (like $\alpha = 1$), check if it satisfies the Armijo condition, and if not, shrink it by a fixed factor (multiply by $\rho$, say $\rho = 0.5$). Keep shrinking until Armijo is satisfied.

Why start with $\alpha = 1$? Because for Newton's method and quasi-Newton methods, $\alpha = 1$ is the "natural" step size; it corresponds to the full Newton step. Often $\alpha = 1$ works, and backtracking never shrinks at all. When it does shrink, it quickly finds an acceptable step size.
Algorithm: Backtracking Line Search
Input: initial step size $\bar{\alpha} \gt 0$ (typically $\bar{\alpha} = 1$), shrink factor $\rho \in (0, 1)$ (typically $\rho = 0.5$ or $0.8$), Armijo parameter $c_1 \in (0, 1)$ (typically $c_1 = 10^{-4}$)
Step 1: Set $\alpha \leftarrow \bar{\alpha}$
Step 2: While $f(x_k + \alpha p_k) \gt f(x_k) + c_1 \alpha \nabla f(x_k)^T p_k$: ← Armijo fails?
$\alpha \leftarrow \rho \, \alpha$ ← shrink the step size
Step 3: Return $\alpha_k = \alpha$
Convergence guarantee: Backtracking always terminates (assuming $p_k$ is a descent direction). Since $\phi'(0) = \nabla f(x_k)^T p_k \lt 0$, there exists a sufficiently small $\alpha$ such that $\phi(\alpha) \lt \phi(0) + c_1 \alpha \phi'(0)$. As $\alpha \to 0$, the left side approaches $\phi(0)$ while the right side approaches $\phi(0)$ from below, but the left side approaches faster because $\phi(\alpha) \approx \phi(0) + \alpha \phi'(0)$ and $c_1 \lt 1$.

Worked Example: Backtracking Line Search

Suppose we are at $x_k$ with $f(x_k) = 10$, $\nabla f(x_k)^T p_k = -5$ (the directional derivative is $-5$, confirming $p_k$ is a descent direction).

Parameters: $\bar{\alpha} = 1$, $\rho = 0.8$, $c_1 = 10^{-3}$.

The Armijo condition says: accept $\alpha$ if $f(x_k + \alpha p_k) \leq 10 + 10^{-3} \cdot \alpha \cdot (-5) = 10 - 0.005\alpha$.

Trial 1: $\alpha = 1$

Suppose $f(x_k + 1 \cdot p_k) = 12$. (The function went UP; we overshot.)

Armijo threshold: $10 - 0.005(1) = 9.995$

$12 \gt 9.995$? YES → FAIL. Shrink: $\alpha \leftarrow 0.8 \times 1 = 0.8$

Trial 2: $\alpha = 0.8$

Suppose $f(x_k + 0.8 \cdot p_k) = 10.5$. (Still went up, but less.)

Armijo threshold: $10 - 0.005(0.8) = 9.996$

$10.5 \gt 9.996$? YES → FAIL. Shrink: $\alpha \leftarrow 0.8 \times 0.8 = 0.64$

Trial 3: $\alpha = 0.64$

Suppose $f(x_k + 0.64 \cdot p_k) = 9.2$. (Now the function decreased.)

Armijo threshold: $10 - 0.005(0.64) = 9.9968$

$9.2 \leq 9.9968$? YES → PASS!

Accept $\alpha_k = 0.64$

We evaluated $f$ three times. The backtracking algorithm found an acceptable step size without solving any optimization problem; just a few function evaluations and comparisons.

Part E: When to Use Which Condition

Condition What It Guarantees Typical Use Guarantees $s_k^T y_k \gt 0$?
Armijo only Sufficient decrease Backtracking with GD, trust region comparison No
Goldstein Sufficient decrease + not too short Newton's method (when no quasi-Newton update needed) Not guaranteed
Wolfe Sufficient decrease + slope flattening BFGS, L-BFGS, quasi-Newton methods Yes
Strong Wolfe Wolfe + controlled uphill slope BFGS (preferred), nonlinear CG Yes

Cost Comparison (from Quiz 3)

Backtracking (Armijo): Only needs function evaluations. Cheapest per iteration. Typical: 1-5 function evaluations per line search.

Wolfe line search: Needs both function and gradient evaluations. More expensive per call, but the stronger guarantee means the overall method converges in fewer outer iterations.

Exact line search: Minimizes $\phi(\alpha)$ exactly. Most expensive per call, but sometimes available in closed form for quadratics (recall the formula $\alpha_k = g_k^Tg_k / (g_k^TAg_k)$ from Page 06).

Bottom line: For quasi-Newton methods (BFGS, L-BFGS), use Strong Wolfe. For steepest descent with backtracking, Armijo alone is fine. For CG, use Strong Wolfe with $c_2 = 0.1$.

Key Relationships to Remember

  • Strong Wolfe $\implies$ Wolfe $\implies$ Armijo. (Each condition is stricter than the previous.)
  • $c_1 \lt c_2$ is required (if $c_1 \geq c_2$, no step size could satisfy both conditions simultaneously).
  • Typical values: $c_1 = 10^{-4}$, $c_2 = 0.9$ (Newton/BFGS) or $c_2 = 0.1$ (CG).
  • For BFGS to work: need $s_k^T y_k \gt 0$ → need Wolfe conditions → use Strong Wolfe for safety.
  • Backtracking only checks Armijo, so it is not suitable for BFGS (unless you add a curvature check).
Summary: The Line Search Fits Into Every Method

Line search is not a standalone algorithm; it is a component used inside other methods:

Glossary

Line Search
The process of finding a good step size $\alpha_k$ along a given search direction $p_k$. Reduces the multi-dimensional problem to a 1D search along the ray $x_k + \alpha p_k$.
$\phi(\alpha) = f(x_k + \alpha p_k)$
The one-dimensional slice of $f$ along the search direction. All line search conditions are expressed in terms of $\phi$.
Descent Direction
A direction $p_k$ such that $\nabla f(x_k)^T p_k \lt 0$, guaranteeing that small positive steps decrease $f$.
Armijo Condition (Sufficient Decrease)
$f(x_k + \alpha p_k) \leq f(x_k) + c_1 \alpha \nabla f(x_k)^T p_k$. Ensures the step makes at least a minimum amount of progress. Prevents too-long steps.
Wolfe Conditions
Armijo + curvature condition $\phi'(\alpha) \geq c_2 \phi'(0)$. Prevents both too-long and too-short steps. Required for BFGS.
Strong Wolfe Conditions
Armijo + $|\phi'(\alpha)| \leq c_2|\phi'(0)|$. Absolute-value version that also limits uphill slope at the new point. Preferred for BFGS and CG.
Goldstein Conditions
Symmetric bounds: $(1-c)\alpha\phi'(0) \leq \phi(\alpha) - \phi(0) \leq c\alpha\phi'(0)$. Simpler than Wolfe but less commonly used.
Backtracking
Start with $\alpha = \bar{\alpha}$, repeatedly multiply by $\rho \in (0,1)$ until Armijo is satisfied. Simple, cheap, always terminates.
$c_1$ (Armijo parameter)
Typically $10^{-4}$. Controls how much decrease is "sufficient." Small value = lenient (easy to satisfy).
$c_2$ (Curvature parameter)
Typically $0.9$ for Newton/BFGS, $0.1$ for CG. Controls how flat the slope must be. Must satisfy $c_1 \lt c_2 \lt 1$.
$\rho$ (Backtracking shrink factor)
Typically $0.5$ or $0.8$. Each failed trial multiplies $\alpha$ by $\rho$, shrinking the step.
$s_k^T y_k \gt 0$ (Curvature Condition for BFGS)
Required by the BFGS update formula. Guaranteed by the Wolfe conditions. This is WHY BFGS uses Wolfe line search.
